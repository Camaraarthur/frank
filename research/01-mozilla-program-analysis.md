## 2. What Exactly They're Looking For

### Three Focus Categories

**Category 1: Enable Better Information**
Systems that diversify information flows, verify content across languages/contexts, reveal algorithmic influence, prevent bot manipulation, and facilitate consensus-building. Success = communities access trustworthy, verifiable information with algorithmic transparency.

**Category 2: Build Institutional Transparency & Accountability**
Government tracking systems, public data infrastructure, accountability mechanisms, deliberation platforms, and participatory decision-making tools. Success = citizens can monitor how power operates and influence governance decisions.

> "Democracy requires transparency about how power operates... technologies that make government and institutional decision-making visible, trackable, and accountable to the people affected by those decisions."

**Category 3: Protect & Expand Civic Space**
Freedom of expression tools, privacy-preserving coordination, propaganda resistance, surveillance-resistant infrastructure, civic education access. Success = organizers coordinate safely despite threats.

### Our best fit: Category 2, with strong overlap into Category 1.

The program page explicitly lists "participatory decision-making tools" that "integrate public input directly into governance decisions" and "community input systems" that "translate public voices into actionable insights for policymakers." This is almost literally what we're building.

## 3. Evaluation Criteria (Six Dimensions)

1. **Technical viability and team execution capacity** -- Is this functional technology with real momentum? Is the technical approach appropriate for the problem, budget, and grant term?
2. **Problem clarity and user dialogue evidence** -- Does the team have a clear understanding of the problem and who they're solving it for? Is there evidence of dialogue with potential users? "Are they pursuing a real need rather than a solution in search of a problem?"
3. **Charitable impact potential and community benefit**
4. **Values alignment** -- transparency, agency, openness
5. **Sustainability pathway plausibility**
6. **Theme fit** -- measurable democratic improvements

**Portfolio-level considerations:** geographic distribution, category diversity, and variety of technical approaches.

## 4. Eligibility & Requirements

### Project Stage (CRITICAL)
> "A working technology that people can actually use -- not just an idea or wireframe."

They want functional, early-stage projects with demonstrated traction. Prototype stage is insufficient -- it must be something people are actually using. But it is early stage; they call it the "Build Track."

### Team
- Any size, as long as it's "sufficient to take forward the work you propose effectively"
- Must be able to execute over 12 months without significant hiring during the grant period
- Must be able to "hit the ground running"

### AI as Core (CRITICAL)
> AI must be "a core part of their solution."

They explicitly reject "a chatbot that leverages one of the big four LLMs" but welcome "new approaches to problems facing democracy that leverage AI or machine learning" in core infrastructure. They take "a broad view of AI, which includes everything from LLMs and agentic AI to small models and traditional machine learning techniques."

### Openness Commitment
- At minimum, some code must be open source (open core model, closed codebase with open datasets, etc.)
- OR teams must commit to open-sourcing part of their codebase during the grant
- Public learnings, datasets, and documentation are required regardless

### Legal Entity
- US-based 501(c)(3) nonprofits
- Non-US nonprofits with 501(c)(3) equivalent legal status
- For-profit social enterprises considered case-by-case (funding must be "narrowly tailored to a specific, identifiable charitable project")
- Fiscal sponsorship is accepted and recommended for orgs without 501(c)(3) status

### Geography
- Single-location projects eligible if you explain why localization is intentional OR describe pathways to broader expansion
- Global South applicants are encouraged (cohort balance considers geographic distribution)

### Language
- Applications in English. Translation tools are welcome. "They evaluate your ideas, not your language quality."

## 5. Mozilla Foundation's Broader Worldview

### The Problem They See

Mozilla frames the current moment as a crisis where:
- "A handful of platforms now control how we build, learn, connect and communicate" (Nabiha Syed)
- "Generative tools produce convincing propaganda at scale"
- "Recommendation algorithms amplify division"
- "Automated systems make consequential decisions about information visibility without transparency or accountability"
- Democratic safeguards can't adapt quickly enough
- There's an erosion of "shared reality" required for democratic discourse

### Their Theory of Change

Mozilla believes the problem isn't just bad actors -- it's structural. The AI ecosystem is "powered exclusively by the market" and "will prioritize a narrow set of profitable applications, leaving critical work... under-resourced relative to its societal value." Their answer: expand who builds AI and for what purpose.

> "A better technology future is underpinned by infrastructure and systems that open and empower, not obscure."

Key phrase from Lindsey Dodson, Director of Global Grants: "We're backing a new generation of technologists pushing back against extractive defaults."

### Five Trustworthy AI Values
1. **Privacy** -- people choose how their data is used
2. **Fairness** -- systems that are "power aware and seek to minimize harm"
3. **Trust** -- "agency and control over data and algorithmic outputs"
4. **Safety** -- strong data protection, awareness of resource extraction
5. **Transparency** -- mandatory transparency so people understand how systems work

### The "Public AI" Concept
Mozilla advocates for "Public AI" -- a counterbalance to market-driven development. They argue commercial AI under-resources critical applications, citing as examples: "using AI to detect illegal mining operations, facilitate deliberative democracy, and deliver medicine directly to patient homes." They want to expand who has "the power and opportunity to build AI in the first place."

### Nabiha Syed's Personal Philosophy
- Arrived as Executive Director in May 2024
- Frames this as returning to Mozilla's foundational question: "Why do we care about the open internet, the open web?"
- "Making sure that public benefit and private enrichment are in balance, that we're centering human beings"
- Advocates for "laws on data access and data transparency, as well as meaningful open source for AI, not just open model weights"
- Positions Mozilla as "pro-human, which is not anti-business"
- Cites behavioural change: people now actively reject surveillance and unwanted AI integration

## 6. Past Cohorts & What Succeeded

### Inaugural Incubator Cohort (2025) -- Environmental Focus
Four projects were funded:

1. **Code Carbon** (France) -- Measures software carbon footprints during development
2. **EyeClimate / MethaneMapper** (USA) -- Detects gas leaks from satellite imagery, giving communities power to "demand accountability"
3. **Fundacion Via Libre** (Argentina) -- Algorithmic auditing tools for non-technical communities
4. **Proyecto Respira** (Paraguay) -- Community-driven air quality monitoring and prediction

### Patterns in Successful Projects
- **Community empowerment angle**: Every project puts data/tools directly in community hands, not just in expert hands
- **Accountability framing**: Tools that let citizens hold power to account (not just inform them)
- **Geographic diversity**: France, USA, Argentina, Paraguay -- clear Global South inclusion
- **Technical specificity**: Each project has a clear, narrow technical approach to a defined problem
- **Open data/open code**: Aligned with Mozilla's openness values

### The Democracy Award (Separate Program)
Mozilla also ran a "Creative Futures Counterstructures" Democracy Award -- distinct from the Incubator but same values DNA. This signals that democracy-focused work is a priority across the Foundation, not just this one cohort.

## 8. The Competitive Landscape & Standout Strategy

### What most applicants will probably propose
Based on the three categories and current AI-democracy discourse:
- Misinformation detection tools
- Fact-checking systems
- Algorithmic transparency dashboards
- Deliberation platforms (like Polis clones)
- Bot detection / election integrity tools
- Content moderation alternatives

### Why our angle is different

The civic tech field has an insight problem articulated by the RethinkAI collaborative: the movement fell into an "efficiency trap, focusing on making government faster and cheaper rather than better." As they note: **"Residents weren't impressed by faster bureaucracies; they wanted different bureaucracies, more human ones, more accountable ones."**

Most AI-for-democracy tools focus on the OUTPUT side of democracy: better voting, better deliberation, better information access. Almost nobody is working on the INPUT side: what happens before deliberation, before voting, before policy proposals exist. The gap is in systematic listening -- understanding what communities actually need and translating that into the language of policy.

### The "wow" framing

**Democracy has a listening problem, not a participation problem.**

This reframe is powerful for Mozilla because:

1. **It's structurally different from other applicants.** Most will pitch "better participation tools." We're saying participation isn't the bottleneck -- listening is. When a city council meeting has 30 speakers repeating variations of the same 3 concerns, the problem isn't that people aren't showing up. It's that no one is systematically synthesising what they're hearing and connecting it to actionable policy.

2. **It maps directly to Mozilla's values.** Their language is about "agency," "centering human beings," and "translating public voices into actionable insights for policymakers" (literally from their program description). Our tool does exactly this -- it doesn't replace human voice, it amplifies it.

3. **AI is genuinely core, not decorative.** The synthesis step -- taking hundreds of unstructured resident interviews and extracting policy-relevant patterns grounded in local government capacity -- is something only AI can do at scale. This isn't a chatbot wrapper. It's NLP, thematic analysis, policy mapping, and evidence synthesis as core infrastructure.

4. **It fills the "Public AI" gap Mozilla identifies.** Using AI to "facilitate deliberative democracy" is literally one of their three examples of under-resourced applications in the Public AI paper. Our tool sits adjacent to this -- it makes the raw material for deliberation richer and more representative.

5. **It bridges researchers and communities in a way that creates accountability.** Researchers do the interviews (human contact), AI does the synthesis (scale), and the output is policy proposals that local government can actually act on (accountability loop). The community isn't just consulted -- they generate the evidence base.

### How to frame for Mozilla specifically

Use their language:
- "Infrastructure of listening" (not "survey tool" or "data platform")
- "Community voices into actionable policy" maps to their "translate public voices into actionable insights for policymakers"
- Frame as "civic infrastructure" not "civic tech"
- Emphasise the accountability loop: input -> synthesis -> proposal -> action -> feedback
- Position against "extractive defaults" -- current civic engagement is extractive (take people's time, produce reports that sit on shelves)
- Stress that this is "power aware" -- it shifts analytical power from consultants/think tanks to communities themselves

### Risks to address proactively
- **"Is this just a survey tool?"** -- No. The AI synthesis layer is what makes it infrastructure, not a tool. Surveys collect data. This generates policy proposals grounded in evidence.
- **"Working technology" requirement** -- Must demonstrate something people can actually use today, beyond wireframes.
- **Open source commitment** -- Need a clear plan for what will be open (datasets? methodology? synthesis models?)
- **Sustainability beyond the grant** -- Need a plausible 12-month path. Government contracts? University partnerships? Foundation funding pipeline?

## 9. Application Strategy Recommendations

### Lead with the reframe
Open with: "Democracy's crisis isn't that people don't want to participate. It's that institutions can't listen." This immediately distinguishes from the 90% of applications that will start with "misinformation is a threat to democracy."

### Hit every evaluation criterion explicitly
1. **Technical viability**: Working tool, specific AI approach (NLP for interview synthesis, policy-mapping models, local government capacity databases)
2. **Problem clarity / user dialogue**: Evidence of conversations with researchers, local government officials, community organizations
3. **Charitable impact**: Direct benefit to underserved communities whose voices are systematically excluded from policy
4. **Values alignment**: Open methodology, transparent synthesis, human agency in every step (AI assists, doesn't decide)
5. **Sustainability**: Path to ongoing use -- municipalities, research institutions, civic organizations as long-term partners
6. **Theme fit**: Category 2 (Institutional Transparency & Accountability) with spillover into Category 1 (Better Information)

### Emphasise what makes AI core
The pitch isn't "we use AI." It's "this problem was previously unsolvable without AI." No human team can synthesize 500 resident interviews into policy proposals grounded in local government capacity in a week. AI makes the listening-to-action pipeline possible at the speed democracy requires.

### Geographic framing
If the project works in one location, explain why -- and describe how the methodology generalises. "We're building the infrastructure in [city], but the listening methodology is location-agnostic. The AI adapts to any local government context."

### Open source commitment
Offer to open-source the synthesis methodology and policy-mapping framework, even if the full platform stays proprietary. Share datasets of community issues (anonymised) and the analytical pipeline. This maps to Mozilla's "open core model" suggestion.
