## Step 2: Conceptual Territories

### A) THE ACT — What is the researcher physically doing?
Walking neighborhoods. Knocking on doors. Sitting on porches. Standing at bus stops. Asking questions. Listening. Recording. Making notes. Covering ground. Going door to door. Doing the rounds. Canvassing. Taking soundings. Gathering testimony.

The word that unifies all of these: **canvassing** — the original civic act of going out to learn what people think.

### B) THE OUTPUT — What does the tool produce?
- A briefing (for the decision-maker)
- A sounding (a measurement of depth/feeling)
- A portrait (of a community's needs)
- A ground truth (verified reality vs. assumption)
- A field report (structured intelligence)
- An evidence base (for policy)

The output is a **briefing grounded in field evidence** — not data, not surveys, but structured human testimony turned into actionable intelligence.

### C) THE GAP IT FILLS — What's broken?
- **The silence**: People speak, nobody with power listens
- **The distance**: Between residents and the officials who make decisions about their lives
- **The fog**: Decision-makers operating on assumptions, not evidence
- **The disconnect**: Policy made without input from those it affects
- **The absence**: Nobody shows up to ask

The gap: **Nobody is doing the listening work properly, or at all.**

### D) THE METAPHOR — What is this like?
- **Taking soundings**: Nautical — measuring depth before navigating. You take soundings before making decisions. Rich, precise, implies care and professionalism.
- **Doing the rounds**: A doctor, a watchperson, a community figure — someone who regularly checks in. Warm, dutiful, human.
- **Canvassing**: Political/civic — going door to door to learn what people think. The most direct civic metaphor.
- **Reading the room**: Understanding what's really going on, not what's assumed.
- **Fieldwork**: Academic/anthropological — rigorous, respectful, systematic.
- **Groundwork**: The essential foundation before anything is built.
- **Doorstep conversations**: The literal act. Where democracy happens informally.

### E) THE FEELING — What should saying the name evoke?
- **Trust**: This is serious, not a toy
- **Warmth**: This is about people, not data
- **Groundedness**: Connected to the real, the local, the specific
- **Craft**: There's skill and care involved
- **Duty**: This work matters and someone ought to be doing it
- **Accessibility**: Not exclusive, not elite — anyone can do this

## Step 6: Top 7 Ranked with Detailed Reasoning

### 1. CANVASS

**What it means**: To canvass is to go systematically through an area, seeking opinions, support, or information. From the original meaning: to examine carefully, to sift through. Related to "canvas" — the surface on which a picture is made.

**Why it works for THIS product**: This is _exactly_ what the tool does. The user canvasses a community — they research it, walk it, talk to people, and build a structured picture. "Canvass" is the single English word that most precisely describes the entire workflow: research, listen, record, synthesize. It is inherently civic (canvassing is what you do before elections, what organizers do, what census-takers do). It implies going TO people rather than asking them to come to you. It also carries the beautiful secondary meaning of "canvas" — a surface onto which a community portrait is painted.

**How it sounds**: "Go to canvass.community" — flows naturally. Two crisp syllables before .community. The double-s gives it a satisfying finish. It sounds active, serious, and purposeful.

**Risks/downsides**: (1) Could be confused with Canvas (the LMS by Instructure) — but the .community TLD and civic context differentiate clearly. (2) Some may associate "canvassing" only with political campaigns — but this is a feature, not a bug; the tool IS civic. (3) Spelling: people might type "canvas" (one s) — consider securing both if possible.

**Cross-cultural check**: "Canvass" exists primarily in English. No negative connotations in other languages. In Spanish, "canvass" doesn't have a direct cognate. In French, "canvasser" means to solicit votes — perfectly aligned. No negative meanings found in any major language.

**Domain**: canvass.community — **AVAILABLE**

---

### 2. SOUNDINGS

**What it means**: Soundings are measurements of depth, originally nautical — dropping a weighted line to understand what lies beneath the surface. "Taking soundings" means carefully measuring before you act. Also used in journalism and governance: "I've been taking soundings" = I've been listening to what people think.

**Why it works for THIS product**: The metaphor is perfect. The tool helps you take the measure of a community — to understand what lies beneath the surface of public discourse. Soundings implies depth, care, methodology, and the act of listening (sound). It positions the tool as rigorous but not cold. The plural form suggests multiple data points, multiple conversations — a composite picture.

**How it sounds**: "Go to soundings.community" — elegant, three syllables, slightly formal. Has a maritime/exploratory quality. Sounds like something a serious person would use.

**Risks/downsides**: (1) Three syllables is slightly long. (2) Might feel too metaphorical — people may not immediately grasp the connection to civic listening. (3) "Soundings" is the name of a UK academic journal (Soundings: A Journal of Politics and Culture) — niche overlap but not a blocker. (4) Could feel too poetic or literary for a practical tool.

**Cross-cultural check**: "Sondage" in French means both "sounding" and "opinion poll" — excellent resonance. "Sondeo" in Spanish has the same dual meaning. No negative connotations found.

**Domain**: soundings.community — **AVAILABLE**

---

### 3. DOORSTEP

**What it means**: The literal threshold of a home — where democracy happens informally, where conversations with residents take place. "On the doorstep" means in the field, talking to real people. "Doorstep conversations" are a staple of community organizing and local journalism.

**Why it works for THIS product**: It is viscerally specific. You can picture it. It captures the physical act at the heart of the tool — going to where people are, standing at their door, and asking them what matters. It implies respect (you go to THEM), accessibility (everyone has a doorstep), and honesty (doorstep conversations are informal, real). It also has the connotation of "on our doorstep" — the issues that are right here, right now.

**How it sounds**: "Go to doorstep.community" — two syllables, very natural. Warm, concrete, human. Easy to remember, easy to spell, easy to say.

**Risks/downsides**: (1) "Doorstepping" in British journalism has a negative connotation — ambushing someone at their home for an unwanted interview. This is a meaningful concern in UK markets. (2) Could feel too literal/small — does it capture the AI analysis and policy synthesis side? (3) Might position the tool as only about in-person interviews, missing the research/briefing component.

**Cross-cultural check**: "Doorstepping" is negative in British English (aggressive journalism). This is a real risk if operating in the UK. Neutral/positive in American English. No direct equivalent in other languages.

**Domain**: doorstep.community — **AVAILABLE**

### 4. ROUNDS

**What it means**: "Doing the rounds" — what a doctor does checking on patients, what a watchperson does patrolling, what a community figure does staying in touch. Also: a round of conversations, a round of listening. Implies regularity, duty, care, and presence.

**Why it works for THIS product**: It captures the practice nature of the work — this isn't a one-off survey, it's a discipline. You do your rounds. It implies that listening is ongoing, that a community deserves regular check-ins. It's warm (the doctor doing rounds is caring), dutiful (the watchperson is responsible), and implies coverage (you cover your rounds). Short, memorable, and feels like a practice rather than a product.

**How it sounds**: "Go to rounds.community" — one syllable + .community. Very quick, very clean. Easy to say, easy to remember. Has a satisfying roundness (pun intended) to the sound.

**Risks/downsides**: (1) "Rounds" is a popular card game on Steam (ROUNDS) — different context but some name-space collision. (2) Could be confused with medical rounds (doctor's rounds) — not necessarily negative. (3) Plural form means you can't easily verb it: "I'm going to round" doesn't work as well as "I'm going to canvass." (4) Somewhat generic — doesn't immediately scream "civic listening."

**Cross-cultural check**: "Rounds" is universally understood in English. "Ronde" in French (patrol, rounds) has positive connotations — a night watchman's rounds. No negative meanings found.

**Domain**: rounds.community — **AVAILABLE**

### 5. GROUNDWORK

**What it means**: The foundational work that must happen before anything is built. The essential, often invisible labor of preparation. "Doing the groundwork" means putting in the effort to understand before acting.

**Why it works for THIS product**: It positions community listening as _foundational_ — the work that must happen before policy, before programs, before decisions. It implies that this is the serious, essential, unglamorous work that makes everything else possible. It connects to "ground truth" (verified field data) and "on the ground" (where real life happens). It honors the work as important.

**How it sounds**: "Go to groundwork.community" — three syllables, compound word. A bit heavy before .community (both are compound-feeling words). But strong, solid, grounded.

**Risks/downsides**: (1) "Groundwork" is the name of several existing organizations (Groundwork USA, Groundwork UK — both environmental/community nonprofits). Real name collision risk. (2) Compound word before compound TLD (.community) feels slightly heavy in speech. (3) Doesn't specifically evoke listening — could be prep work for anything.

**Cross-cultural check**: No negative connotations. Translates well conceptually across languages.

**Domain**: groundwork.community — **AVAILABLE** (but note existing nonprofits with this name)

### 6. WITNESS

**What it means**: To witness is to see and hear something firsthand, and to attest to what you observed. A witness is someone who was there, who can testify. It implies presence, attention, and truth.

**Why it works for THIS product**: It captures the most powerful aspect of the tool — bearing witness to what a community actually experiences. It elevates the work from data collection to something more human: the act of being present, paying attention, and faithfully recording what people say. It implies truth-telling and accountability. "Witness" positions the user as someone who takes the responsibility of accurate representation seriously.

**How it sounds**: "Go to witness.community" — two syllables, strong, serious. Has gravity. Commands respect.

**Risks/downsides**: (1) Strong religious connotations — Jehovah's Witnesses, "bearing witness" in Christian tradition. Could alienate secular audiences or create unintended associations. (2) Legal connotations — witness testimony, court witnesses. (3) Passive: a witness observes but doesn't necessarily act. The tool is about action (policy proposals), not just observation. (4) Slightly heavy/somber — lacks the warmth desired.

**Cross-cultural check**: "Witness" has strong religious associations globally (Jehovah's Witnesses operate in most countries). "Testigo" in Spanish, "temoin" in French — both carry legal weight. The religious association is a real concern.

**Domain**: witness.community — **AVAILABLE**

### 7. GLEAN

**What it means**: To glean is to gather grain left behind after a harvest — to collect valuable things that others have overlooked or left behind. By extension: to gather information bit by bit, carefully, from scattered sources.

**Why it works for THIS product**: It perfectly captures what the tool does with community intelligence — gathering the valuable insights that are scattered across a community, the things people say that nobody in power has bothered to collect. It implies patience, care, and the discovery of value where others see nothing. It has a beautiful agricultural warmth to it. Short, one syllable, ancient word.

**How it sounds**: "Go to glean.community" — one syllable, clean, quick. Flows well. Has a gentle quality.

**Risks/downsides**: (1) Many people don't know what "glean" means — it's somewhat archaic/literary. Requires explanation, which is a problem for a name. (2) Glean.com is an existing enterprise AI search company (well-funded, well-known in tech). Significant name collision risk, even with .community TLD. (3) Passive/gentle — might not convey the structured, rigorous nature of the tool. (4) Could sound like a data harvesting tool, which has negative connotations.

**Cross-cultural check**: "Glaner" in French carries the same beautiful meaning. "Espigar" in Spanish likewise. No negative connotations, but low recognition in non-English contexts.

**Domain**: glean.community — **AVAILABLE** (but note collision with Glean.com, the enterprise AI company)

## Recommendation

**Canvass** is the strongest candidate by a meaningful margin. Here is why:

1. **Semantic precision**: It is the only candidate that describes the ENTIRE workflow — researching an area, going to people, asking questions, gathering opinions, building a picture. Every other name captures only one facet.

2. **Civic DNA**: Canvassing is an inherently democratic act. It doesn't need to be explained or metaphorized — people understand that canvassing is how you learn what a community thinks.

3. **Active voice**: It's a verb. "I'm canvassing." "We canvassed the neighborhood." "The canvass shows..." It works as noun AND verb, which is rare and valuable for a product name.

4. **The double meaning**: Canvass (to survey) + Canvas (the surface on which a picture is painted). The tool creates a canvas — a community portrait built from field evidence.

5. **Scale-neutral**: Canvassing works for one person with a phone or a team of fifty. It doesn't imply any particular scale of operation.

6. **canvass.community** — says exactly what it is. No explanation needed. No clever wordplay required.

The main risk (confusion with Canvas LMS) is mitigated by context: nobody looking for civic field research will accidentally end up on a learning management system, and the .community TLD makes the civic positioning clear.

**Register canvass.community immediately.** Consider also securing canvass.app and canvas.community (one s) as protective registrations if budget allows.
