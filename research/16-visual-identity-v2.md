# Visual Identity v2: Frank — Raw, True, Transparent

> Previous research (15-visual-identity.md) recommended Libre Baskerville (serif) for typography. **Rejected.** The direction is now: raw, informal, transparent, sans-serif. "It's not about looking trustworthy or official, it's about being raw and true."

This document builds on that rejection. Everything here is oriented toward a Frank that feels like raw civic data given form — not a newspaper, not a government site, not a startup.

### Three Models That Emerge

**Model 1: "The Evidence" (ProPublica, Marshall Project, Bellingcat, TBIJ)**
- Near-zero decoration. Black/white/one accent.
- Typography does all the work. Content IS the brand.
- The message: "We don't dress things up. Here are the facts."
- Colors: black, white, one sharp accent (yellow, red, or none).

**Model 2: "The Institution" (GOV.UK, Transparency International, Open Government Partnership)**
- Blue-dominant. Clean, accessible, professional.
- The message: "We are reliable and authoritative."
- Colors: blue, white, black, cautious accents.

**Model 3: "The Community" (De Correspondent, mySociety, Full Fact)**
- Warmer palette. Multiple accent colors. Illustrations.
- The message: "We are friendly, approachable, inclusive."
- Colors: warm neutrals, teal/coral/pink/yellow.

### Where Frank Should Be

Frank is not an institution (Model 2). Frank is not purely warm and friendly (Model 3). **Frank is closest to Model 1 — "The Evidence" — but with warmth.**

The Marshall Project is the single best precedent: they use monospace typography as a brand statement that says "this is raw data, this is transparent, you can see the source." But they pair it with a serif for editorial warmth. That combination — raw + warm — is exactly what Frank needs.

### Color Psychology: Raw Truth vs. Institutional Trust vs. Warm Community

| Feeling | Colors That Create It | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Raw truth / transparency** | White or near-white, black, single sharp accent | Minimal palette = nothing to hide. No decoration = no spin. |
| **Institutional trust** | Blues, grays, conservative palette | Blue is universally the "trust" color. Restraint = reliability. |
| **Warm community** | Warm neutrals (beige, cream), earth tones, coral, teal | Warmth = human presence. Imperfect colors = imperfect humans. |
| **Urgency / accountability** | Red, yellow/amber on dark | Alert colors. News tickers. Warning signs. |
| **Transparency (literal)** | White space, minimal fills, open layouts, visible structure | Transparency = you can see through it. Less decoration = more visible structure. |

**Key insight:** True transparency in design is not about a specific color — it is about *showing the structure*. A transparent design lets you see the grid, the hierarchy, the source of the data. Monospace type, visible data labels, source citations, timestamps — these communicate transparency more than any color can.

### Warm vs. Stark: The Central Question

The current Frank palette (beige `#EFEBE3`, brown `#492615`, muted navy `#464C72`, vermilion, gold) is warm. It reads as editorial, crafted, considered. This is good for trust-building and approachability.

But the user's instinct is toward something more raw. The question is: **can you be warm AND raw?**

**Yes.** Here is how:
- Keep a warm background (not pure white) but strip away all decorative color use
- Use a sans-serif (not serif) to remove "editorial elegance"
- Use monospace for data, citations, and system elements — this is the rawness signal
- Restrict accent colors to functional use only (links, actions, status) — no decorative color
- Let whitespace and typography create warmth instead of color warmth

## 2. Typography for "Raw and True"

### What the Best Organizations Use (and Why)

| Organization | Headline Font | Body Font | Data/System Font | Why It Works |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **ProPublica** | Graphik (geometric sans) | Tiempos (serif) | — | Sans headline = modern authority. Serif body = editorial depth. |
| **The Marshall Project** | Miller Display (serif) | — | **GT Pressura Mono** | Monospace is the brand statement. Says: "this is evidence, not opinion." |
| **The Guardian** | Guardian Egyptian (slab serif) | Guardian Text Sans | — | Custom type IS the brand. Recognizable instantly. |
| **Bellingcat** | Sans-serif | Georgia (dropcaps) | — | Deliberately plain. The investigations are dramatic; the design is not. |
| **GOV.UK** | GDS Transport (humanist sans) | GDS Transport | — | One font for everything = maximum clarity, zero pretension. |
| **Hacker News** | Verdana | Verdana | — | The most "raw" design on the internet. Zero design = zero spin. |

### Four Typography Approaches Ranked for Frank

#### Approach 1: RECOMMENDED — Grotesque Sans + Monospace

**The concept:** A clean grotesque sans-serif for all reading text (headlines, body, UI). A monospace for all data, citations, sources, timestamps, and system information. The grotesque says "direct, no-nonsense." The monospace says "raw data, nothing hidden."

**Why this is right for Frank:**
- Grotesque sans-serifs (the family including Helvetica, Akzidenz Grotesk, and their descendants) are the most "neutral" typefaces. They do not editorialize. They present information without commentary. This IS frankness.
- Monospace type is the language of terminals, code, raw data, plain text. It signals: "this has not been processed or prettified."
- The Marshall Project proved this combination works for investigative/civic content.
- The contrast between proportional sans (warm, readable) and monospace (raw, systematic) creates natural visual hierarchy without needing serif/sans contrast.

**Google Fonts implementation:**

| Role | Font | Weight | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Headlines** | **Space Grotesk** | 500-700 | Derived from Space Mono — shares DNA with the monospace. Geometric but with character. Not as sterile as Inter, not as corporate as Helvetica. Has quirky details (curved 'R', angled terminals) that give it personality without being decorative. |
| **Body text** | **Space Grotesk** | 400 | Same family = cohesion. Open counters, good x-height, reads well at 16px. |
| **Data / citations / sources / timestamps** | **Space Mono** | 400, 700 | The monospace sibling of Space Grotesk. Same design DNA. Used for: resident quotes in data view, source labels, timestamps, council response codes, data tables. |
| **UI / small labels** | **Space Grotesk** | 400-500 | Navigation, buttons, form labels. |

**Why Space Grotesk + Space Mono specifically:**
- They are from the same family (designed by Florian Karsten, based on Colophon Foundry's Space Mono). This means they share proportions, x-height, and design language.
- Space Grotesk has personality that Inter, DM Sans, and other "safe" grotesques lack. Its slightly quirky letterforms (the capital R, the number 1, the tail of the lowercase a) give it character without being distracting. It feels *authored*, not *generated*.
- Space Mono is one of the best monospace fonts on Google Fonts — readable, distinctive, and designed for screen.
- Both are free, open-source, and well-hinted for screen rendering.

**Alternative grotesque options (all Google Fonts):**

| Font | Character | Good For | Concern |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Inter** | Neutral, clean, everywhere | Maximum readability | Too ubiquitous — looks like every SaaS app. Zero personality. |
| **DM Sans** | Geometric, modern | Clean UI | A bit cold. Lacks the character that "frank" needs. |
| **Work Sans** | Warm, slightly quirky | Body text, UI | More personality than Inter. Good fallback option. |
| **Manrope** | Semi-condensed, geometric | Dashboards, data-dense layouts | Space-efficient. Good for data views. |
| **IBM Plex Sans** | Corporate-humanist | Body text | Already in current Frank. Very good but reads as "corporate." |
| **Outfit** | Modern geometric | Headlines, UI | Clean and confident. Less personality than Space Grotesk. |

#### Approach 2: GOOD — Single Humanist Sans (One Font Family)

**The concept:** One humanist sans-serif for everything. No monospace, no serif. Pure simplicity. Like GOV.UK with GDS Transport.

**Google Fonts pick:** **Work Sans** or **Source Sans 3**

Work Sans has warmth — it was designed for screen body text at 14-16px with open counters. It has personality without being quirky. Source Sans 3 (Adobe's open-source workhorse) is more neutral but extremely well-crafted.

**Why it might work:** Maximum simplicity. Nothing to distract. The rawness comes from the *absence* of typographic variety, not from the presence of monospace.

**Why it is less good than Approach 1:** Without a monospace for data elements, Frank loses the visual signal that says "this is raw data." Everything looks the same, whether it is a resident's quote, a council response, or a navigation label. The grotesque + monospace pairing creates meaningful visual differentiation.

#### Approach 3: INTERESTING — Monospace for Everything

**The concept:** Monospace for ALL text. Headlines, body, data, everything. Like a terminal. Like WikiLeaks. Like plain text.

**Google Fonts pick:** **IBM Plex Mono** or **JetBrains Mono**

**Why it is tempting:** This is the most "raw" possible approach. It says: "we are showing you the data exactly as it exists. No prettification."

**Why it is probably wrong:** Monospace is significantly harder to read for body text. It takes up more horizontal space. It signals "developer tool" rather than "civic platform." Most residents will not find it inviting. It works for Hacker News because that audience self-selects for technical comfort. Frank needs to be accessible to a mosque elder, a teenager, a council officer, and a resident who has never used anything beyond Facebook.

**Possible compromise:** Use monospace ONLY for data-heavy views (tables, timelines, response tracking) and grotesque sans for everything else. This is essentially Approach 1.

#### Approach 4: REJECTED — Serif Headlines

This was the previous direction (Playfair Display, Libre Baskerville, IBM Plex Serif). Rejected by the user. Serif headlines feel editorial, institutional, "trustworthy in a newspaper way." The user wants something more direct.

### Font Size and Scale Recommendations

| Element | Size | Weight | Font | Line Height |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| H1 (page title) | 32px / 2rem | 700 | Space Grotesk | 1.2 |
| H2 (section) | 24px / 1.5rem | 600 | Space Grotesk | 1.25 |
| H3 (subsection) | 20px / 1.25rem | 600 | Space Grotesk | 1.3 |
| Body text | 16px / 1rem | 400 | Space Grotesk | 1.6 |
| Small / caption | 14px / 0.875rem | 400 | Space Grotesk | 1.5 |
| Data / code / source | 14px / 0.875rem | 400 | Space Mono | 1.5 |
| Data label | 12px / 0.75rem | 700 | Space Mono | 1.4 |
| Button text | 16px / 1rem | 500 | Space Grotesk | 1 |

### Line Length

50-70 characters per line for body text. This is non-negotiable for readability. On desktop, this means body text should sit in a column no wider than ~680px at 16px.

## 3. Logo Concepts for "Frank" as an Adjective

### Guiding Principles

1. Frank is an adjective, not a person. No anthropomorphism.
2. The logo must work at 16x16px (favicon) and at billboard scale.
3. It must communicate: direct, honest, unpolished-but-intentional, data-driven, civic.
4. Google Fonts only (for the wordmark type).
5. It must feel raw-but-warm, not raw-and-cold.

### Research: What Works for Data/Transparency Tool Logos

| Tool | Logo Type | What Works | What Doesn't |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Bellingcat** | Lowercase wordmark + cat illustration | The name + visual metaphor is memorable. But the cat is a specific reference (bell the cat). | The illustration adds charm but would not work for Frank — too figurative. |
| **ProPublica** | Clean uppercase wordmark | Pure typography. The name does all the work. Serious, institutional, recognizable. | Could feel too corporate for Frank. |
| **mySociety** | CamelCase wordmark | Simple, recognizable. The camelCase adds a tech-friendly informality. | Feels dated (2000s web convention). |
| **Full Fact** | Simple wordmark | Clean, direct, no symbol needed. The two-word name with alliteration is the brand. | Not very distinctive — could be any fact-checking org. |
| **Pol.is** | Lowercase with dot | The period/dot is meaningful — it is a URL, a statement, a punctuation mark. Minimal. | Almost too minimal — hard to remember or recognize in isolation. |
| **GOV.UK** | Uppercase with crown | Crown = authority. The period = a URL. Instantly recognizable in the UK. | The crown is government-specific — not applicable. |

### Seven Logo Concepts, Evaluated

#### Concept A: Pure lowercase "frank" in Space Grotesk

```
frank
```

Set in Space Grotesk Bold (700), lowercase, tight tracking (-0.02em).

**Strengths:**
- Maximum simplicity. The name IS the brand.
- Space Grotesk's quirky details (the 'r' with its angled terminal, the 'k' with its asymmetric arms) give it subtle personality.
- Lowercase = approachable, informal, not shouting.
- Immediately readable at any size.

**Weaknesses:**
- No distinctive visual hook. Hard to identify in a sea of wordmarks.
- The favicon would just be "f" — not distinctive enough.

**Verdict:** Strong foundation, but needs one more element.

#### Concept B: "frank" with quotation marks as brand element

```
"frank"
```
or
```
"frank
```
(open quote only — the conversation is never closed)

**Strengths:**
- Quotation marks instantly communicate: this platform surfaces what people *actually said*.
- The open-quote-only variant is conceptually powerful — Frank is an ongoing conversation, not a closed statement.
- A large open-quote mark (") works beautifully as a favicon and app icon.
- Typographically distinctive — few brands use punctuation as a brand element.
- In Space Grotesk, the quotation marks have a slightly geometric, characterful shape.

**Weaknesses:**
- Could be confused with "a person named Frank" in quotation marks (ironic or sarcastic reading).
- The open-quote-only concept requires explanation — though once explained, it is memorable.

**Verdict: STRONGEST CONCEPT.** The open quote is the brand mark. It means: "We are listening. This is what was said. The conversation is open."

#### Concept C: frank with an equals sign / balance symbol

```
frank =
```
or
```
= frank
```

**Strengths:**
- Equals sign = equal access, equity, mathematical precision, accountability.
- Has a code/programming feel (variable assignment: `frank = truth`).

**Weaknesses:**
- The equals sign is heavily associated with the marriage equality campaign (HRC logo). Could create unintended associations.
- Feels more like a tech/programming reference than a civic one.
- Does not directly relate to Frank's core function (listening, surfacing, accountability).

**Verdict:** Interesting but too ambiguous. The equals sign carries too much existing meaning.

#### Concept D: frank with a cursor/text cursor

```
frank|
```
or
```
frank_
```

A blinking cursor after "frank" — always updating, always live, always recording.

**Strengths:**
- Signals "live data," "always on," "being written right now."
- The cursor is the universal symbol for "input" — Frank takes input from the community.
- Works well in digital contexts (could literally blink on the website).
- Has a raw, terminal-like quality.

**Weaknesses:**
- Blinking cursor is hard to render in static contexts (print, favicon, social cards).
- The underscore variant (`frank_`) looks like a filename or variable name — too technical.
- Could suggest "under construction" or "incomplete."

**Verdict:** Good as a secondary animation/interaction detail (the cursor blinks on the homepage hero) but not as the primary logo.

#### Concept E: [frank] in brackets

```
[frank]
```

Square brackets like a citation, a reference, an annotation, a data field.

**Strengths:**
- Brackets say: "this is a reference," "this is cited," "this is from a source."
- Academic/journalistic connotation of rigor and citation.
- Has a raw, code-like quality: `[frank]` looks like a variable, a placeholder, a command.
- The brackets frame the word, suggesting containment and structure.

**Weaknesses:**
- Could read as "optional" (brackets in grammar mean optional content).
- Could read as a programming array or placeholder — too technical for a civic audience.
- The brackets make the logo wider and busier than it needs to be.
- As a favicon, `[f]` is not great.

**Verdict:** Interesting conceptually but visually cluttered. Better as a secondary brand element (e.g., source citations in the app use `[source]` formatting) than as the primary logo.

#### Concept F: "frank" with one letter in a different color

```
frank    (where the 'a' is in red/vermilion)
```

**Strengths:**
- Creates a visual focal point.
- The highlighted letter could have meaning (the 'a' for "accountability"? the 'r' for "raw"?).

**Weaknesses:**
- Arbitrary. Why that letter? The meaning has to be explained, which means it does not communicate on its own.
- Color-dependent — fails in monochrome contexts.
- Trendy technique (Flickr did this in the 2000s) that has aged.

**Verdict:** Weak. Do not pursue.

#### Concept G: Simple geometric mark (circle, square)

**Strengths:**
- A circle = community, wholeness, inclusion.
- A square = framework, structure, foundation.

**Weaknesses:**
- Too abstract. A circle or square has no inherent connection to Frank's mission.
- Every tech company has a geometric mark. Frank would disappear.
- Requires building meaning from scratch — years of brand-building to make a circle mean "Frank."

**Verdict:** Reject. Frank's name is strong enough to carry the brand. It does not need an abstract symbol.

### RECOMMENDED LOGO DIRECTION

**Primary mark:** `"frank` — lowercase in Space Grotesk Bold (700), with a single open-quote mark before the word. No closing quote. Tight tracking.

**The open quote** is rendered slightly larger than standard punctuation (about 120% of cap height) and in the accent color (vermilion `#E62B1E` or slate navy `#464C72`), while "frank" is in the text color.

**Favicon / app icon:** The open-quote mark alone ( " ) — large, bold, in the accent color on the background color. Simple, recognizable, meaningful.

**Monochrome version:** All one color (dark brown `#492615` on light, white `#EFEBE3` on dark).

**Why this works:**
- The open quote is an *invitation to speak*. Frank is a listening platform.
- An unclosed quotation is an *open conversation*. It never ends.
- The quote mark is a universal symbol — no cultural or linguistic barrier.
- It scales from 16px favicon to billboard.
- It is typographically elegant without being decorative.
- It is not a person, not figurative, not anthropomorphic.

## 4. The "Handmade" Question

### Can You Have Raw AND Handmade?

**Short answer:** Yes, but the handmade element must be *raw-handmade*, not *cute-handmade*.

### The Spectrum of Handmade

```
Cute/Craft         Raw/Field           Clean/System
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Illustrated icons   Rough sketches      Geometric icons
Rounded shapes      Uneven lines        Sharp edges
Pastel colors       High contrast       Monochrome
Playful layout      Notebook grid       Rigid grid
Children's charity  War correspondent   Government portal
                    notebook
```

**Frank should be in the "Raw/Field" zone** — if handmade elements are used at all.

### What "Raw Handmade" Looks Like

- **Not** cute illustrated icons with rounded corners and pastel fills (like Notion or Duolingo)
- **Not** polished hand-lettering (like a craft brewery or artisan brand)
- **Yes** rough, quick marks — like annotations in the margin of a document
- **Yes** hand-drawn underlines, circles, arrows — like a journalist marking up a source
- **Yes** redline-style marks — like editing, annotating, fact-checking

### The Better Answer: Rawness Through Structure, Not Illustration

The user's instinct to move away from hand-drawn is correct. Here is why:

1. **Hand-drawn elements add a layer between the user and the data.** Frank's power is that it shows you the raw reality — what people said, what the council did, what the numbers show. Every illustration, every decorative element, every hand-drawn icon is a filter that says "someone interpreted this for you."

2. **The rawness should come from the DATA being visible, not from the style being rough.** Show timestamps. Show source labels. Show the number of people who said something. Show the council's response (or lack thereof). Show the data pipeline. This is real rawness — not aesthetic rawness.

3. **Monospace type for data elements IS the handmade signal.** Monospace type looks like someone typed it on a typewriter. It looks like code. It looks like a terminal readout. It has all the "raw" energy of a hand-drawn style but without the risk of looking cute or unprofessional.

### Recommendation

**Do not use hand-drawn icons or illustrations in the core product.**

Instead, achieve rawness through:
- Monospace type for data, sources, timestamps, and citations
- Visible structure (grids, borders, clear hierarchy — nothing hidden behind smooth surfaces)
- Minimal decoration (no gradients, no shadows, no border-radius beyond 2-4px)
- Source labels visible everywhere ("From: Community Survey, March 2026. N=347.")
- Timestamps on everything ("Last updated: 4 hours ago")
- Plain language (no corporate euphemism, no "engagement metrics" — just "347 people said this")

If illustration is ever needed (e.g., for an empty state or onboarding), use **simple line drawings** — single-weight stroke, no fill, no color, like technical diagrams. Not cute. Functional.

### Option C: Warm Stark (The Synthesis)

**Philosophy:** The best of both worlds. A very slightly warm background (barely perceptible, not the strong beige of Option A). Near-black text for maximum authority. Vermilion as the single strong accent. The warmth is *there* but so subtle it reads as "careful consideration" rather than "craft." The monospace data layer provides rawness.

This is inspired by how Bellingcat uses a near-white with subtle cream accents, and how De Correspondent uses warm colors without ever feeling unprofessional.

| Error | Deep red | `#C4221A` | 5.24:1 (AA) | Form errors, alert states |

**Font pairing:**
- Headlines + body: **Space Grotesk** (400, 500, 600, 700)
- Data / citations / system: **Space Mono** (400, 700)

**Logo treatment:** `"frank` in Space Grotesk 700, dark charcoal `#2C1D12`. Open quote in vermilion `#D42B1E`.

**Mood board:**
- *Feels like:* A modern newsroom whiteboard. Clean but alive. Structured but not sterile. The kind of space where serious work happens and you can see the evidence pinned to the wall.
- *Real-world object:* A well-lit architecture studio. A modern council chamber after a renovation — clean, warm wood, good lighting, no dark corners. A research lab that also has plants.
- *Who trusts it:* Almost everyone. It is warm enough for a community member who is anxious about interacting with government. It is clean enough for a council officer who needs it to look credible in a presentation. It is modern enough for a teenager who would dismiss a GOV.UK-style site as boring. It is raw enough (with the monospace data layer) for a journalist who wants to feel like they are looking at real data.
- *Why this might be the best option:* It threads the needle. The background warmth is subliminal — you feel it but do not notice it. The near-black text has authority but not coldness (the brown undertone prevents that). The single accent color (vermilion) is the strongest Frank has — warm, urgent, human. The monospace data layer provides all the rawness needed.
- *Risk:* Could feel like "trying to please everyone" if not executed with conviction. The subtlety of the warm-white could be lost on screens with poor color accuracy. Needs to be tested on real devices.

## 6. Detailed Mood Boards

### Option A: Warm Beige ("The Field Notebook")

**Visual references:**
- Manila folders and kraft paper
- A journalist's Moleskine notebook full of shorthand
- The Pentagram "Frank" publication identity
- De Correspondent's warm digital palette
- Old newspaper classified sections (dense, warm, textured)

**App feel:**
When you open Frank, it feels like opening a worn-in notebook that someone has been keeping for your community. The beige background feels like paper. The dark brown text feels like ink. The monospace data (Space Mono) feels like typewriter output — a transcript of what was actually said. The vermilion accent marks feel like a red pen annotation: "this is important."

Navigation is clean and structural. Cards have subtle warm-gray borders. Data tables use monospace and feel like printouts. Resident quotes are indented with a large open-quote mark in vermilion. Council responses have a different background (slightly lighter beige) and a monospace source label.

**The feeling:** Trustworthy through warmth. "Someone is keeping careful track of this. Someone cares."

**Who uses it:**
- A resident opens it on their phone and feels: "this is for me, not for the council."
- A council officer opens it on their laptop and feels: "this is well-organized and credible."
- A journalist opens it and feels: "this is a primary source I can cite."

### Option B: Stark Clean ("The Exhibit")

**Visual references:**
- ProPublica longform investigations
- A courtroom exhibit board
- GOV.UK service pages
- WikiLeaks document releases
- Terminal/command-line output made readable

**App feel:**
When you open Frank, it feels like opening a well-organized dossier. White background, black text, everything laid out with ruthless clarity. Nothing decorative. No warmth — but no coldness either, because the content itself is human (resident voices, community data). The vermilion accent is the only color, and it means something every time it appears: "pay attention to this."

Data views are dense and clean. Monospace (Space Mono) is used for source labels, timestamps, and data tables — it feels like a database made readable. The contrast between the clean sans-serif headlines and the monospace data creates a rhythm: editorial framing, then raw evidence, then editorial framing.

**The feeling:** Trustworthy through transparency. "There is nothing hidden here. This is all of it."

**Who uses it:**
- A journalist opens it and feels: "this is a credible source."
- A researcher opens it and feels: "I can use this data."
- A resident opens it and feels: maybe slightly intimidated. It looks "official." But once they see their own words reflected back, they feel heard.
- A teenager might bounce. It looks like homework.

### Option C: Warm Stark ("The Studio")

**Visual references:**
- Bellingcat's website (clean with subtle warmth)
- A Scandinavian design studio (minimal, warm materials)
- The Guardian's online layout (structured, authoritative, not cold)
- A well-designed public library — modern, bright, welcoming, serious
- Apple's educational materials (clean but human)

**App feel:**
When you open Frank, it feels like walking into a well-lit, modern civic space. The background is warm enough to feel welcoming but clean enough to feel professional. The dark text is authoritative. The vermilion accent draws your eye to what matters.

The monospace data layer provides moments of rawness — when you see a resident's quote in Space Mono with a timestamp and source label, it feels like evidence. When you see the council's response (or lack of one), the monospace formatting gives it a "record of fact" quality. But the overall environment is warm and inviting.

Cards are clean with subtle warm-gray borders. Hover states are gentle. The open-quote favicon sits in the browser tab like a quiet reminder: "we are listening."

**The feeling:** Trustworthy through clarity AND warmth. "This is a serious tool, but it was made by people who care about you."

**Who uses it:**
- A resident opens it on their phone and feels: "this is clean and easy to use." Not intimidating, not childish.
- A mosque elder opens it and feels: "this is respectful and clear."
- A teenager opens it and feels: "this looks modern enough that I will give it a chance."
- A council officer opens it and feels: "this is credible. I can share this in a meeting."
- A journalist opens it and feels: "this is a useful, well-structured source."

## 7. Final Recommendation

### The Pick: Option C (Warm Stark) as primary, with Option A's warmth as fallback

**Option C is the strongest** because it maximizes trust across the widest range of users without sacrificing Frank's personality. The subtle warmth prevents it from feeling like a government tool. The clean structure prevents it from feeling like a craft project. The monospace data layer is where the rawness lives.

### Summary of the Complete Visual Identity

**Typography:**
- Primary: **Space Grotesk** (Google Fonts) — 400, 500, 600, 700
- Data/citations/system: **Space Mono** (Google Fonts) — 400, 700
- Scale: 32/24/20/16/14px hierarchy, 1.6 line height for body

**Colors (Option C):**
- Background: `#FAF9F6` (warm white)
- Text: `#2C1D12` (dark charcoal-brown) — 15.42:1 contrast
- Secondary text: `#5C4F44` — 7.18:1 contrast
- Links: `#3A4063` (deep slate navy) — 8.12:1 contrast
- Accent/CTA: `#D42B1E` (vermilion) — 5.02:1 contrast
- Highlight: `#FFF8E7` (pale gold background)
- Data labels: `#ECEDF2` (pale slate background)
- Success: `#1A7A4C` / Error: `#C4221A`

**Logo:**
- Mark: `"frank` — Space Grotesk 700, open-quote in vermilion, wordmark in charcoal
- Favicon: Large open-quote mark ( " ) in vermilion on warm white
- Monochrome: all charcoal or all white

**Icons/Illustration:**
- No hand-drawn icons in core product
- Minimal, single-weight line icons for UI (outline style, 1.5-2px stroke)
- Rawness comes from monospace type and visible data structure, not from illustration style

**Design principles:**
1. Show the structure. Nothing hidden behind smooth surfaces.
2. Monospace for data. If it came from a source, it looks like a source.
3. One accent color. Vermilion means "pay attention."
4. Generous whitespace. Confidence, not clutter.
5. Accessible first. Every color pairing passes AA minimum. Body text passes AAA.
