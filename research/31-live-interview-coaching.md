# Live Interview Coaching System for Frank's Recording Page

**Date:** 2026-03-18
**Purpose:** Design a real-time coaching system that surfaces adaptive question suggestions during field interviews, integrated into the existing recording page.

---

## 1. What Makes a Good Civic Interview Question

### The Core Problem

Most interview questions produce bad data. "What do you think about housing?" generates opinions. "Tell me about the last time you tried to find a place to live" generates stories. Stories contain burden. Opinions contain performance.

Three frameworks converge on the same insight:

### 1.1 The Mom Test (Fitzpatrick)

Rob Fitzpatrick's three rules, adapted for civic listening:

1. **Talk about their life, not your idea.** Never mention Frank, the app, policy, or the council's position. Ask about their morning, their week, their route to work.
2. **Ask about specifics in the past, not generics about the future.** "Tell me about the last time you had to deal with the council" -- not "What do you think the council should do?"
3. **Talk less, listen more.** The researcher's job is to hold space, not fill it.

The Mom Test identifies three categories of worthless data:
- **Compliments** -- "That sounds like a great project" tells you nothing about their burden.
- **Hypothetical fluff** -- "I would definitely use that" is noise. Only past behaviour is signal.
- **Wishlists** -- Feature requests and policy opinions are the researcher projecting. Stories of what happened are ground truth.

**The key question transform:**

| Bad (opinion-seeking) | Good (experience-seeking) |
|---|---|
| What do you think about housing? | Tell me about the last time you tried to find a place to live. |
| Do you feel safe here? | Walk me through your journey home last night. |
| What should the council do about bins? | What happened the last time your bin wasn't collected? |
| Is transport good here? | How did you get here today? How long did it take? |
| Do you like your neighbourhood? | What does a typical Saturday look like for you here? |

### 1.2 Freire's Dialogical Method

Paulo Freire's critical pedagogy provides the philosophical grounding:

- **Reject the banking model.** Traditional surveys treat residents as vessels to be emptied of data. The resident is the expert on their own experience.
- **Dialogue, not extraction.** The interview is a mutual encounter, not a data collection exercise.
- **Listen for generative themes.** Freire's method involves listening for the characteristic language through which people name their daily struggles. These "generative themes" -- recurring words, phrases, emotional inflections -- are the data.
- **Problem-posing, not answer-seeking.** Pose open situations ("Tell me about what's been difficult this month") rather than closed questions that presuppose a framework.

**Critical distinction for Frank:** Freire's educational method seeks transformation. Frank's research method seeks understanding. The researcher must not attempt to raise consciousness, politicise, or educate during the interview. The goal is to hear what is already there.

### 1.3 Phenomenological Interviewing

From the qualitative research tradition:

- Focus on first-person experience of civic burden.
- Use open-ended questions designed to elicit description, not evaluation.
- Seek the "lifeworld" -- concrete, day-to-day reality.
- Follow narrative arcs when they emerge. Do not redirect to the interview guide.
- Respect silences. Wait at least 5 seconds after a participant finishes before probing. The most valuable disclosures often come after an initial pause.

## 2. Question Bank: 30 Questions Organised by Conversation Stage

These questions are designed for field interviews lasting 5-15 minutes with residents in public spaces (markets, bus stops, pubs, high streets). The stages overlap -- the interviewer should follow the conversation, not the script.

### Stage 1: Opening (0-60 seconds) -- Warm Up, Establish Rapport

The goal is to make the person comfortable talking. No civic content yet. Just two humans having a conversation.

1. **"How's your day going?"** -- Universal opener. Low stakes. Shows you see them as a person, not a data source.
2. **"Have you been in [area] long?"** -- Establishes connection to place without demanding specifics.
3. **"What brought you here today?"** -- If at a market, bus stop, or pub. Contextual and natural.
4. **"What's this area like to live in?"** -- Broad, open, invites them to set the frame.
5. **"What does a typical week look like for you around here?"** -- The Mom Test question. About their life, not your agenda.

### Stage 2: Exploring (1-3 minutes) -- Broad, Open-Ended, About Their Life

The goal is to surface the topics that matter to them. The researcher does not introduce topics. The resident does.

6. **"What's changed most around here in the last couple of years?"** -- Invites temporal perspective. Often surfaces loss, development, displacement.
7. **"What takes more of your time or energy than it should?"** -- Direct burden question. Surfaces friction without naming a domain.
8. **"Is there anything about living here that frustrates you?"** -- Open frustration prompt. Let them choose the topic.
9. **"What do your neighbours talk about most?"** -- Shifts perspective from individual to communal. Reduces self-consciousness.
10. **"If someone was thinking about moving here, what would you tell them -- honestly?"** -- The "honest" qualifier signals you want the real answer, not the polite one.
11. **"What works well here? What do people get wrong about this area?"** -- Balance. Avoids deficit framing. Also surfaces pride and identity.
12. **"Walk me through what your morning looks like."** -- Concrete, specific, experiential. Surfaces transport, childcare, work, commute burdens without asking about any of them directly.

### Stage 3: Deepening (3-8 minutes) -- Follow Up on What They Mentioned

The goal is to go deeper on the topics they raised. These are follow-up templates, not standalone questions.

13. **"You mentioned [X] -- can you tell me more about that?"** -- Basic deepening probe. Shows you are listening.
14. **"Tell me about the last time that happened."** -- The Mom Test's most powerful question. Moves from general complaint to specific story.
15. **"Walk me through what you did. What happened next?"** -- Narrative probe. Gets the full sequence, which reveals systemic friction.
16. **"How long has that been going on?"** -- Temporal probe. Distinguishes acute incidents from chronic burdens.
17. **"Have you tried to do anything about it?"** -- Reveals agency, exhaustion, and institutional response (or lack thereof).
18. **"What happened when you contacted them?"** -- Institutional interaction probe. Often surfaces the gap between stated policy and lived experience.
19. **"Is that something other people around here deal with too, or is it just you?"** -- Communal validation. Also reveals whether the burden is isolating.
20. **"You said [exact word they used] -- what do you mean by that?"** -- Echo probe. Uses their language. Prevents researcher projection.

### Stage 4: Probing (8-12 minutes) -- Root Causes, Systemic Questions

The goal is to move from symptoms to structures. Use these only when the resident has already opened the door to deeper reflection.

21. **"Why do you think that is? What's behind it?"** -- Causal probe. Invites structural analysis from the resident's perspective.
22. **"Is there anything you've given up trying to fix?"** -- Surfaces learned helplessness and institutional failure. Often the most revealing question.
23. **"When you say 'they don't care,' can you tell me about a specific time that showed you that?"** -- Specificity probe. Converts generalisation into evidence.
24. **"What would have to change for that to get better?"** -- Future-oriented but grounded in their identified problem, not yours.
25. **"Is there something everyone around here knows but nobody talks about?"** -- The hidden burden question. Surfaces taboo topics (crime, drugs, housing conditions, domestic violence, institutional racism).
26. **"Who would you say has the power to change that?"** -- Power mapping from the resident's perspective. Reveals their mental model of governance.

### Stage 5: Closing (last 1-2 minutes) -- Wrap Up, Return Agency

The goal is to end well. Leave the resident feeling heard, not interrogated.

27. **"Is there anything I should have asked about but didn't?"** -- Returns control. Often surfaces the thing they have been wanting to say the whole time.
28. **"If you could change one thing about this area, what would it be?"** -- Concrete, bounded, forward-looking. A satisfying closing question.
29. **"Anything else you want to add?"** -- Simple. Open. Respectful.
30. **"Thank you. That was really helpful. Is there anything you said that you'd rather I didn't include?"** -- Continuous consent. Ethical closing. The resident has final editorial control.

## 3. Conversation Stage Detection

The coaching system must detect which stage the conversation is in to serve appropriate questions. This is done by a combination of elapsed time and transcript content analysis.

### Time-Based Defaults

| Elapsed Time | Default Stage | Shift Trigger |
|---|---|---|
| 0:00 - 1:00 | Opening | Move to Exploring when resident mentions a place, experience, or topic |
| 1:00 - 3:00 | Exploring | Move to Deepening when a specific topic is mentioned twice, or emotional language appears |
| 3:00 - 8:00 | Deepening | Move to Probing when institutional actors are named, or systemic language appears |
| 8:00 - 12:00 | Probing | Move to Closing when energy drops, or researcher has enough |
| 12:00+ | Closing | Always available |

### Content-Based Signals

Gemini should detect these signals in the transcript to determine stage transitions:

**Move to Deepening when:**
- A topic is mentioned more than once ("the bins", "the council", "the rent")
- Emotional intensifiers appear ("really frustrating", "it's terrible", "I've had enough")
- A specific incident is referenced ("last week", "the other day", "when I went to")

**Move to Probing when:**
- Institutional actors are named ("the council", "the landlord", "the school", "the NHS")
- Systemic language appears ("it's always been like this", "nothing ever changes", "they never")
- The resident generalises from personal to communal ("everyone here", "we all")

**Move to Closing when:**
- The conversation has been going for 10+ minutes
- The resident's turn lengths are getting shorter
- The same topic is being repeated without new information

## 4. The Gemini Prompt Template for Live Follow-Up Questions

This prompt is sent to Gemini every 45-60 seconds with the latest transcript chunk. It must be fast (use gemini-flash) and focused (return exactly 2-3 questions).

### System Prompt (sent once at session start)

```
You are a live interview coaching assistant for a civic field researcher. The researcher is conducting a semi-structured interview with a resident about their experience of living in their area. Your job is to suggest 2-3 follow-up questions based on what has been said so far.

RULES:
1. Questions must be about THEIR LIFE, not about policy opinions. Never suggest "What do you think the council should do?" or similar.
2. Questions must ask about SPECIFICS IN THE PAST. "Tell me about the last time..." not "Do you think..."
3. Questions must be SHORT -- max 15 words each. The researcher needs to glance at these, not read paragraphs.
4. Questions must follow from what the RESIDENT said, not from what the RESEARCHER asked.
5. Never suggest leading questions, closed yes/no questions, or questions that presuppose a political framing.
6. If the resident mentioned something emotionally charged, suggest ONE gentle follow-up on that topic.
7. If the resident mentioned something and then moved on quickly, suggest a question that returns to it.
8. If the resident has been talking about the same topic for a while, suggest a question that goes DEEPER (root cause, specific incident, who was involved).

CONVERSATION STAGE: {stage}
- If Opening: suggest broad, warm questions about their life and area.
- If Exploring: suggest questions that follow up on topics they introduced.
- If Deepening: suggest questions that ask for specific incidents, timelines, and institutional interactions.
- If Probing: suggest questions about root causes, power, and systemic patterns.
- If Closing: suggest "anything else?" style questions and questions that return agency.

AREA: {area}

Return ONLY a JSON array of 2-3 strings. No explanation, no preamble.
Example: ["Tell me about the last time that happened.", "How long has that been going on?", "Have you tried to do anything about it?"]
```

### Per-Chunk Prompt (sent every 45-60 seconds)

```
LATEST TRANSCRIPT (last 60 seconds):
"""
{transcript_chunk}
"""

FULL CONVERSATION SUMMARY SO FAR:
- Topics mentioned: {topics_list}
- Emotional moments: {emotional_flags}
- Unexplored threads: {unexplored_threads}
- Time elapsed: {elapsed_mm_ss}
- Current stage: {stage}

Generate 2-3 follow-up questions for the researcher. Return ONLY a JSON array of short question strings.
```

### Running Summary Maintenance

To avoid sending the entire transcript every time (which would be slow and expensive), maintain a running summary on the client side:

```typescript
interface ConversationState {
  topics: string[];           // ["housing", "bin collection", "school"]
  emotionalMoments: string[]; // ["resident raised voice about landlord", "long pause after mentioning mum"]
  unexploredThreads: string[];// ["mentioned sister's experience but moved on", "started to say something about the park"]
  currentStage: "opening" | "exploring" | "deepening" | "probing" | "closing";
  elapsedSeconds: number;
  lastChunkText: string;      // raw text of the last 60 seconds
}
```

This state is updated locally after each Gemini response (Gemini can return both questions AND an updated state).

## 5. Golden Rules: Always Visible on Screen

These four rules should be permanently visible on the recording screen as a small, muted panel. They are the interviewer's north star.

### The Four Rules

```
1. Their life, not your idea
   Ask about their experience. Never mention Frank, policy, or what you think.

2. Specifics in the past
   "Tell me about the last time..." not "What do you think about..."

3. Listen more than you talk
   Silence is productive. Wait 5 seconds before following up.

4. They are the expert
   You are here to learn, not to lead. Follow their story.
```

### Things to NEVER Say

These should be accessible via a single tap on a "Never say" label, expanding to show:

```
- "Don't you think the council should..."  (leading)
- "Do you feel like [political framing]..."  (projecting)
- "Would you use an app that..."  (product validation)
- "On a scale of 1-10..."  (quantifying lived experience)
- "What do you think should be done about..."  (opinion-seeking)
- "Do you agree that..."  (closed, leading)
```

### Handling Difficult Moments

Accessible via a "If things get difficult" label:

```
PARTICIPANT BECOMES UPSET:
  Pause. "I can see this is difficult. Thank you for sharing."
  "Would you like to take a break, change subject, or stop?"

PARTICIPANT DISCLOSES SOMETHING SENSITIVE:
  Listen. Do not express shock.
  "That's really helpful. I'd anonymise that before including it anywhere."

AWKWARD SILENCE:
  Wait. Count to 5 in your head. Do not rush to fill it.
  The best disclosures come after pauses.
```

### Body Language Reminders

Accessible via a "Body language" label:

```
- Eye contact, not screen contact. Glance at prompts, don't read them.
- Nod. Lean in slightly. Show you are listening.
- Keep your phone low or flat. Do not hold it between you.
- Mirror their energy. If they are quiet, be quiet.
- Never cross your arms. Open posture signals openness.
```

The coaching overlay must be non-intrusive. The interviewer should be looking at the person, not the screen. They glance at the phone for 1-2 seconds, see a suggested question, and return to eye contact.

### Design Decisions

**Where do suggested questions go?**
Above the mic button, in a translucent panel. Rationale:
- The mic button is the visual anchor. The interviewer's eyes naturally drift there.
- Questions above the mic are in the upper third of the screen -- visible when the phone is held low or placed flat on a table.
- Below the mic would compete with the transcript.
- A separate drawer (pull-up or slide-in) requires a deliberate gesture, which breaks conversation flow.
- An overlay on the map wastes the map space.

**Should they be dismissable?**
No. They update automatically every 45-60 seconds. Dismissing creates interaction burden. Old questions simply fade out and are replaced by new ones. If the interviewer does not need them, they ignore them.

**Should they be swipeable?**
No. Research from the UX document (17-ux-research.md) concluded that swiping is wrong for interview prompts because it encourages rapid consumption, implies binary judgment, and requires interaction during a live conversation. The questions should be a static, glanceable list.

**Font size and opacity:**
- Questions: 14px, `opacity: 0.65` (visible but not dominant)
- Colour: `--frank-gray-700` (#404040) -- readable but not black
- Background: `--frank-white` with `opacity: 0.85` -- slightly translucent so it does not feel like a solid block
- No borders, no cards. Just text floating above the mic.

**Transitions:**
- New questions fade in over 800ms (`opacity: 0 -> 0.65`)
- Old questions fade out over 400ms before new ones appear
- No sliding, no bouncing, no attention-grabbing animation
- The update should feel like a thought forming, not a notification arriving

**The golden rules strip:**
Four small pill labels below the coaching panel, always visible:
- `1. Their life` `2. Past specifics` `3. Listen more` `4. They're expert`
- Font: 11px monospace, `opacity: 0.4`
- Tapping any pill shows a 2-line explanation as a tooltip. No modal, no drawer.

**The reference labels at the bottom:**
Three small text labels below the transcript:
- `Never say` | `If difficult` | `Body language`
- Tapping expands inline (pushes content up) with the reference text
- Tap again to collapse
- These are for pre-interview preparation and mid-interview emergencies, not continuous reference

### Map Adjustment

The map is currently 200px. During recording with the coaching system active, shrink it to 120px. The map is context, not the primary UI. During recording, the primary UI is: coaching suggestions + mic + transcript.

### Pre-Recording State

Before recording starts (consent phase), the coaching panel shows the Stage 1 (Opening) questions as static suggestions:
```
SUGGESTED OPENING QUESTIONS:
- "How's your day going?"
- "Have you been in Beckton long?"
- "What's this area like to live in?"
```

These are not AI-generated -- they are from the static question bank. This gives the researcher something to prepare with while the consent toggles are being confirmed.

## 7. Technical Implementation

### Architecture

```
Browser (Recording Page)
  |
  |-- SpeechRecognition API (Web Speech API, already implemented)
  |     |
  |     +-- transcript entries sent to server via POST /api/sessions/:id/transcript
  |
  |-- Every 45-60 seconds:
  |     |
  |     +-- POST /api/coaching/suggest
  |           Body: { sessionId, transcriptChunk, conversationState }
  |           Response: { questions: string[], updatedState: ConversationState }
  |
  +-- UI updates coaching panel with new questions (fade transition)
```

### New API Endpoint: POST /api/coaching/suggest

```typescript
// server/src/routes/coaching.ts

import { Router } from "express";
import { getModel } from "../gemini";

const router = Router();

interface ConversationState {
  topics: string[];
  emotionalMoments: string[];
  unexploredThreads: string[];
  currentStage: "opening" | "exploring" | "deepening" | "probing" | "closing";
  elapsedSeconds: number;
}

router.post("/suggest", async (req, res) => {
  const { sessionId, transcriptChunk, conversationState } = req.body;

  const systemPrompt = `You are a live interview coaching assistant for a civic field researcher...`; // full prompt from Section 4

  const userPrompt = `
LATEST TRANSCRIPT (last 60 seconds):
"""
${transcriptChunk}
"""

CONVERSATION STATE:
- Topics mentioned: ${conversationState.topics.join(", ") || "none yet"}
- Emotional moments: ${conversationState.emotionalMoments.join(", ") || "none"}
- Unexplored threads: ${conversationState.unexploredThreads.join(", ") || "none"}
- Time elapsed: ${Math.floor(conversationState.elapsedSeconds / 60)}:${String(conversationState.elapsedSeconds % 60).padStart(2, "0")}
- Current stage: ${conversationState.currentStage}

Return a JSON object with:
{
  "questions": ["question 1", "question 2", "question 3"],
  "updatedState": {
    "topics": [...],
    "emotionalMoments": [...],
    "unexploredThreads": [...],
    "currentStage": "..."
  }
}`;

  try {
    const model = getModel();
    const result = await model.generateContent({
      contents: [
        { role: "user", parts: [{ text: systemPrompt + "\n\n" + userPrompt }] }
      ],
      generationConfig: {
        temperature: 0.7,
        maxOutputTokens: 512,
        responseMimeType: "application/json",
      },
    });

    const text = result.response.text();
    const parsed = JSON.parse(text);

    res.json({
      questions: parsed.questions || [],
      updatedState: parsed.updatedState || conversationState,
    });
  } catch (err) {
    console.error("Coaching suggestion error:", err);
    // Fail silently — coaching is non-critical
    res.json({ questions: [], updatedState: conversationState });
  }
});

export default router;
```

### Client-Side Hook: useInterviewCoaching

```typescript
// web/lib/useInterviewCoaching.ts

import { useState, useEffect, useRef, useCallback } from "react";

interface ConversationState {
  topics: string[];
  emotionalMoments: string[];
  unexploredThreads: string[];
  currentStage: "opening" | "exploring" | "deepening" | "probing" | "closing";
  elapsedSeconds: number;
}

interface CoachingState {
  questions: string[];
  state: ConversationState;
  isLoading: boolean;
}

const COACHING_INTERVAL_MS = 45_000; // 45 seconds
const MIN_TRANSCRIPT_LENGTH = 20;    // characters before first suggestion

export function useInterviewCoaching(
  isRecording: boolean,
  elapsed: number,
  transcript: string[],
  sessionId: string | null,
  area: string
) {
  const [coaching, setCoaching] = useState<CoachingState>({
    questions: [],
    state: {
      topics: [],
      emotionalMoments: [],
      unexploredThreads: [],
      currentStage: "opening",
      elapsedSeconds: 0,
    },
    isLoading: false,
  });

  const lastRequestTime = useRef(0);
  const lastTranscriptLength = useRef(0);

  const fetchSuggestions = useCallback(async () => {
    if (!sessionId || !isRecording) return;

    // Get transcript text since last request
    const recentTranscript = transcript.slice(-5).join(" ");
    if (recentTranscript.length < MIN_TRANSCRIPT_LENGTH) return;

    // Don't request if transcript hasn't changed
    if (recentTranscript.length === lastTranscriptLength.current) return;
    lastTranscriptLength.current = recentTranscript.length;

    setCoaching(prev => ({ ...prev, isLoading: true }));

    try {
      const res = await fetch("/api/coaching/suggest", {
        method: "POST",
        headers: { "Content-Type": "application/json" },
        body: JSON.stringify({
          sessionId,
          transcriptChunk: recentTranscript,
          conversationState: {
            ...coaching.state,
            elapsedSeconds: elapsed,
          },
        }),
      });

      if (res.ok) {
        const data = await res.json();
        setCoaching({
          questions: data.questions || [],
          state: data.updatedState || coaching.state,
          isLoading: false,
        });
      }
    } catch {
      // Fail silently -- coaching is non-critical
      setCoaching(prev => ({ ...prev, isLoading: false }));
    }
  }, [sessionId, isRecording, transcript, elapsed, coaching.state]);

  // Periodic suggestion fetching
  useEffect(() => {
    if (!isRecording) return;

    const interval = setInterval(() => {
      const now = Date.now();
      if (now - lastRequestTime.current >= COACHING_INTERVAL_MS) {
        lastRequestTime.current = now;
        fetchSuggestions();
      }
    }, 5000); // Check every 5 seconds, but only request every 45

    return () => clearInterval(interval);
  }, [isRecording, fetchSuggestions]);

  return coaching;
}
```

### How Often to Call Gemini

| Parameter | Value | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Interval | 45 seconds | Fast enough to be responsive, slow enough to not overwhelm. At 15 min interview = ~20 calls max. |
| Model | gemini-flash-latest | Fastest Gemini model. Latency ~1-2 seconds. |
| Max tokens | 512 | Questions are short. The response is a small JSON object. |
| Temperature | 0.7 | Creative enough to vary questions, constrained enough to stay relevant. |
| Fail mode | Silent | If Gemini is slow or fails, the interviewer still has the static question bank and golden rules. Coaching is enhancement, not dependency. |

### What to Send

Each request sends:
- The last ~60 seconds of transcript text (not the full transcript -- privacy and speed)
- A compact conversation state object (topics, emotions, threads, stage)
- The elapsed time
- The area name

What is NOT sent:
- The full transcript (too large, too slow, unnecessary)
- Any participant identifying information
- Audio data (we use the browser's speech recognition, not Gemini's audio API)

### What to Show

Each Gemini response produces:
- 2-3 short question strings displayed in the coaching panel
- An updated conversation state used for the next request

The questions are displayed as plain text, fading in. No attribution ("AI suggests..."), no explanation, no context. Just questions the researcher can glance at.

### Offline / Degraded Mode

If the device has no internet connection (common in field settings):
- The coaching panel shows questions from the static question bank (Section 2), cycling through the appropriate stage based on elapsed time only.
- No Gemini calls are attempted.
- The static bank is bundled in the client JavaScript -- zero network dependency.
- When connectivity returns, live coaching resumes automatically.

## 8. Existing Tools: What They Do and What Frank Can Learn

### Gong.io (Sales Coaching)

Gong does NOT provide real-time coaching during calls. It records, transcribes, and analyses calls post-hoc. Managers review annotated calls, scorecards, and AI-generated feedback after the fact. Their 2025 "Mission Andromeda" launch added AI coaching features, but these are still post-call. For live coaching, Gong suggests pairing with tools like Lindy.

**What Frank can learn:** Post-call analysis is valuable but not what we need. Frank needs the live component that Gong deliberately chose not to build -- because for sales calls, real-time tips can feel robotic. For research interviews, the interviewer IS using a script; live suggestions are natural to the workflow.

### Otter.ai (Live Transcription)

Otter provides real-time transcription with speaker identification, highlights, and collaborative features. The UI shows the transcript flowing as you speak, with the current word highlighted. Key UX: the recording state is always visible -- you never wonder "am I recording?"

**What Frank can learn:** The transcript-as-it-flows UI is already implemented in Frank. Otter does not suggest questions -- it is a transcription tool. Frank's coaching layer goes beyond what Otter offers.

### Quals.ai (AI Qualitative Research)

Quals.ai is an AI-powered qualitative research platform with a proprietary "SmartAsk" engine that dynamically generates follow-up questions during the research process. However, it conducts AI-led interviews (the AI is the interviewer), not human-led interviews with AI coaching.

**What Frank can learn:** The SmartAsk concept validates the approach of dynamically generating follow-up questions from conversation context. But Frank's implementation is fundamentally different: the human is the interviewer, the AI is the whisperer. The questions are suggestions, not commands.

### Interview Copilot Tools (Interviews.chat, Final Round AI, LockedIn AI)

These tools provide real-time answer suggestions to job interview candidates. They listen to the interviewer's question and suggest responses. The UX is: a subtle overlay on screen that updates as the conversation progresses. They work with Zoom, Google Meet, and other platforms.

**What Frank can learn:** The overlay UX pattern is validated. A subtle, non-intrusive panel with 2-3 suggestions that update periodically. The key difference: these tools coach the answerer. Frank coaches the questioner.

### Rev Mobile App (Journalism)

Rev allows journalists to capture, transcribe, edit, and share interviews from the field. Real-time transcription with bookmarking and quote selection. The UX is designed for field conditions -- works in noisy environments.

**What Frank can learn:** Field recording must work offline and handle noise. The existing SpeechRecognition API in the browser is adequate for clear speech but degrades in noisy environments. Consider: should Frank offer audio recording as a backup, with server-side transcription via Gemini's audio API post-interview?

### Gap in the Market

No existing tool combines:
1. Live transcription
2. Real-time AI-generated follow-up questions adapted to conversation context
3. A civic/qualitative research methodology (not sales, not job interviews)
4. Field-first mobile design

This is the gap Frank fills.

## 10. Data Flow Summary

```
1. Researcher taps mic button (consent already given)
   |
2. SpeechRecognition starts, timer starts
   |
3. Transcript entries flow to server (existing: POST /sessions/:id/transcript)
   |
4. Client accumulates transcript locally
   |
5. Every 45 seconds (if new transcript text exists):
   |
   +-- Client sends POST /api/coaching/suggest
   |     with: last 60 seconds of text + conversation state
   |
   +-- Server forwards to Gemini Flash
   |     with: system prompt (methodology rules) + user prompt (transcript + state)
   |
   +-- Gemini returns: 2-3 questions + updated state
   |
   +-- Server returns JSON to client
   |
   +-- Client updates coaching panel (fade in new questions)
   |
6. Repeat until recording stops
   |
7. On stop: final update to session, redirect to results
```

### Cost Estimate

- Gemini Flash pricing: ~$0.075 per 1M input tokens, ~$0.30 per 1M output tokens
- Each coaching request: ~500 input tokens, ~100 output tokens
- Per interview (15 min, ~20 requests): ~10,000 input tokens + ~2,000 output tokens
- Cost per interview: ~$0.001 (less than a tenth of a penny)
- 100 interviews per day: ~$0.10/day

Coaching is essentially free at any reasonable scale.
