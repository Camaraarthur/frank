# Interview Methodology, Research Ethics, and Consent Frameworks for Civic Field Research

## Purpose

This document establishes the methodological and ethical foundations for Beat's civic field research programme. It governs how researchers interview residents about their civic burdens -- not what they think, but what weighs on them. It is designed to satisfy Mozilla Trustworthy AI requirements, GDPR compliance, and the ESRC Framework for Research Ethics.

Beat researchers are not pollsters. They are not surveying opinions. They are listening for the weight of lived experience -- the friction, exhaustion, and quiet injustice that residents carry but rarely articulate. This demands methodological rigour and ethical seriousness.

---

## 1. Core Interview Philosophy: Listening for Burden, Not Opinion

### 1.1 The Mom Test Applied to Civic Listening

Rob Fitzpatrick's _The Mom Test_ articulates three rules that transfer directly to civic field research:

1. **Talk about their life, not your idea.** Never pitch Beat, the app, or a policy position. Ask about their week, their morning, their commute, their last interaction with a government office.
2. **Ask about specifics in the past, not generics or opinions about the future.** "Tell me about the last time you had to deal with [the council / your landlord / the school]" -- not "What do you think about housing policy?"
3. **Talk less, listen more.** The researcher's job is to create space, not to fill it.

Three categories of bad data to guard against:

- **Compliments** -- "That's a great project you're building" tells you nothing about their burdens.
- **Hypothetical fluff** -- "I would definitely use an app like that" is worthless. Past behaviour is the only reliable signal.
- **Wishlists** -- Feature requests and policy opinions are noise. Stories of what happened are signal.

**Adaptation for civic context:** The Mom Test was designed for product validation. In civic research, the danger is not building the wrong product -- it is projecting the researcher's political framing onto the resident's experience. A resident struggling with bin collection frequency is not making a political statement. They are describing the texture of their week.

### 1.2 Paulo Freire's Dialogical Method

Freire's critical pedagogy provides the philosophical grounding for Beat's interview approach:

- **The banking model is the enemy.** Traditional surveys and polls treat residents as vessels to be filled with questions and emptied of data. Freire called this the "banking method" of education. Beat interviews reject this. The resident is the expert on their own experience.
- **Dialogue, not extraction.** Authentic dialogue requires seeing and recognising the personhood of the other and the worlds they inhabit. The interview is a mutual encounter, not a data extraction exercise.
- **Conscientisation, not manipulation.** Freire's concept of _conscientizacao_ -- the process by which people become critically aware of the structures shaping their lives -- must emerge from the resident's own reflection, not from the researcher's prompting. The researcher does not lead the witness.
- **Problem-posing, not answer-seeking.** The researcher poses open situations ("Tell me about what's been difficult this month") rather than closed questions that presuppose a framework.

**Critical distinction:** Freire's method in educational contexts seeks transformation. In Beat's research context, we seek understanding. The researcher must not attempt to raise consciousness, politicise, or educate during the interview. The goal is to hear what is already there.

## 2. Qualitative Research Methods

### 2.1 Methodological Framework: Phenomenological Interviews with Grounded Theory Analysis

Beat's primary method is **phenomenological interviewing** -- understanding the meaning participants place on their lived experience -- analysed through **grounded theory** -- building theoretical understanding from the data rather than testing pre-existing hypotheses.

**Phenomenological interviewing:**
- Focuses on the first-person experience of civic burden
- Requires 5-25 interviews per location to surface common themes
- Uses open-ended questions designed to elicit description, not evaluation
- Seeks the "lifeworld" of the participant -- their concrete, day-to-day reality

**Grounded theory analysis:**
- Theory is "grounded" in data generated with participants who have experienced the process under study
- Coding is emergent, not predetermined
- Researchers must bracket their own assumptions (Husserl's _epoche_)
- Categories emerge through constant comparison across interviews

**Narrative inquiry** supplements these methods when a participant tells a sustained story. The researcher should follow the narrative arc rather than redirecting to the interview guide.

### 2.2 Conversation Analysis Principles

From the academic field of Conversation Analysis (CA), Beat researchers should understand:

- **Turn-taking:** Talk is structurally organised. The researcher must not dominate turns. Silences are productive -- they create space for reflection. Do not rush to fill a pause.
- **Adjacency pairs:** Questions create expectations for answers. Leading questions create expectations for agreement. This is why question design matters so much.
- **Repair sequences:** When a participant corrects themselves or backtracks, this is analytically significant. It often indicates they are working towards an authentic formulation. Do not interrupt repair.
- **Next-turn proof procedure:** How a participant responds to a question reveals how they understood the question. If their answer does not address what you intended, the problem is your question, not their comprehension.

## 3. Interview Guide Design

### 3.1 Structure: Semi-Structured with Funnel Technique

Beat interviews use a **semi-structured** format: a guide with core questions, but flexibility in wording, sequencing, and follow-up.

The **funnel technique** governs question ordering:

**Stage 1: Broad opening (5 minutes)**
- "Tell me about your neighbourhood."
- "What does a typical week look like for you?"
- "How long have you lived here?"

**Stage 2: Experience-focused narrowing (15-20 minutes)**
- "What's been the most frustrating thing you've had to deal with recently?"
- "Tell me about the last time you had to contact [council / landlord / school / GP surgery]."
- "Walk me through what happened."
- "How did that end up?"

**Stage 3: Burden mapping (10-15 minutes)**
- "Is there anything you've given up trying to fix?"
- "What takes more of your time or energy than it should?"
- "Is there something your neighbours all deal with but nobody talks about?"

**Stage 4: Closing and participant control (5 minutes)**
- "Is there anything I should have asked about but didn't?"
- "Is there anything you've said today that you'd rather I didn't include?"
- "Would you be comfortable if I contacted you to check I've understood correctly?"

### 3.2 Probing Techniques

- **Echo probes:** Repeat the participant's last words with a questioning intonation. "The council just... stopped responding?"
- **Summary probes:** "So if I'm understanding, you spent three months going back and forth before anything happened?"
- **Silence:** Wait at least 5 seconds after a participant finishes before probing. Many of the most valuable disclosures come after an initial pause.
- **Specificity probes:** "When you say 'they don't care,' can you tell me about a specific time that showed you that?"
- **Emotional probes (use sparingly):** "How did that make you feel?" -- only when the participant has already signalled emotional content.

### 3.3 Questions to Never Ask

- "Don't you think the council should...?" (leading)
- "Do you feel like [political framing]...?" (projecting)
- "Would you use an app that...?" (hypothetical / product validation)
- "On a scale of 1-10, how would you rate...?" (quantifying lived experience)
- "What do you think should be done about...?" (soliciting opinion, not experience)
- "Do you agree that...?" (closed, leading, opinion-seeking)

## 4. GDPR Compliance for Field Interviews

### 4.1 Lawful Basis for Processing

Under Article 6 of the UK GDPR / EU GDPR, Beat must identify a lawful basis for processing personal data collected during interviews.

**Primary lawful basis: Legitimate Interest (Article 6(1)(f))**

For non-public-sector organisations conducting research, legitimate interest is the most appropriate lawful basis. This requires a documented Legitimate Interest Assessment (LIA) demonstrating:
- The interest is legitimate (civic research to improve democratic participation)
- Processing is necessary (cannot achieve the research purpose without the data)
- The individual's rights do not override (mitigated through safeguards)

**Consent is not the recommended lawful basis for the research processing itself.** The ICO and ESRC are clear: there is no rule requiring consent as the lawful basis for scientific research. Consent in GDPR terms is fragile -- it can be withdrawn at any time, potentially invalidating entire datasets. However, ethical informed consent (see Section 6) remains mandatory as a separate obligation.

### 4.2 Special Category Data (Article 9)

Civic burden interviews will inevitably surface special category data:
- **Racial or ethnic origin** -- through demographic data or self-disclosure
- **Political opinions** -- through discussion of civic experience
- **Religious beliefs** -- through context of place of worship
- **Health data** -- through disclosure of disability, mental health, chronic conditions
- **Trade union membership** -- through employment-related burdens

**Article 9(2)(j)** provides a condition for processing special category data for research purposes, subject to:
- Appropriate safeguards (anonymisation, pseudonymisation, access controls)
- Data minimisation
- A Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA)

### 4.3 Data Minimisation in Practice

| Data Type | Collect? | Justification | Minimisation Approach |
|---|---|---|---|
| Verbatim quotes | Yes | Core research output | Anonymise at transcription |
| Age | Yes (range) | Demographic representativeness | Collect as decade range (30s, 40s) |
| Gender | Yes | Demographic representativeness | Self-identified, free text |
| Ethnicity | Optional | Representativeness checking | Only if participant volunteers |
| Exact address | No | Not needed | Collect ward/neighbourhood only |
| GPS coordinates | Conditional | Location of interview only | Round to 3 decimal places (~100m) then delete precise coordinates |
| Name | No | Not needed for research | Use participant codes (P001, P002) |
| Phone/email | Conditional | Follow-up contact only | Store separately from interview data, delete after follow-up |
| Audio recording | Conditional | Transcription accuracy | Delete after transcription verified |
| Photographs | No | Re-identification risk too high | Do not photograph participants |

### 4.4 GPS Data Handling

GPS data requires particular care because precise geolocation (within ~530 metres / 1,750 feet) is classified as sensitive data under multiple privacy frameworks.

**Protocol:**
1. Record interview location at ward/neighbourhood level for analysis
2. If GPS is recorded for mapping purposes, truncate to 3 decimal places (approximately 100m precision) immediately upon collection
3. Never store GPS coordinates linked to participant identifiers
4. Never display individual interview locations on a public map -- aggregate to area level

### 4.5 Data Subject Rights

Participants must be informed of and able to exercise:
- **Right of access** (Article 15) -- obtain a copy of their data
- **Right to rectification** (Article 16) -- correct inaccuracies
- **Right to erasure** (Article 17) -- request deletion ("right to be forgotten")
- **Right to restrict processing** (Article 18)
- **Right to data portability** (Article 20)

**Practical implementation:** Provide participants with a contact email and participant code at the time of interview. Maintain an erasure log. Respond to requests within one calendar month.

### 4.6 Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA)

A DPIA is mandatory when processing special category data at scale. Beat must complete a DPIA before commencing fieldwork in any new area. The DPIA must cover:
- Nature, scope, context, and purpose of processing
- Necessity and proportionality assessment
- Risk assessment for data subjects
- Measures to mitigate risk
- Consultation with a Data Protection Officer or equivalent

### 4.7 International Considerations

As of 2026, enforcement of GDPR has resulted in over 2,679 fines totalling more than EUR 6.7 billion. Penalties can reach EUR 20 million or 4% of global revenue. This is not an abstract compliance exercise.

The European Commission's Q4 2025 Digital Package proposes the first significant GDPR reform, including SME relief measures. Beat should monitor this for changes to research provisions.

## 5. Research Ethics Frameworks

### 5.1 ESRC Framework for Research Ethics: Six Core Principles

The Economic and Social Research Council (ESRC) Framework for Research Ethics applies throughout the entire research lifecycle -- from planning through design, data collection, analysis, dissemination, publication, and data archiving. Its six principles are:

1. **Benefit and risk management.** Research should aim to maximise benefit for individuals and society and minimise risk and harm.
2. **Respect for rights and dignity.** The rights and dignity of individuals and groups should be respected.
3. **Voluntary and informed participation.** Wherever possible, participation should be voluntary and appropriately informed.
4. **Integrity and transparency.** Research should be conducted with integrity and transparency.
5. **Clear responsibility and accountability.** Lines of responsibility and accountability should be clearly defined.
6. **Research independence.** Independence of research should be maintained and where conflicts of interest cannot be avoided, they should be made explicit.

### 5.2 Ethical Research Without University Affiliation

Beat operates outside the traditional university Research Ethics Committee (REC) / Institutional Review Board (IRB) structure. This does not exempt Beat from ethical review -- it makes it more important to establish credible oversight.

**Options for ethical governance:**

1. **Independent Ethics Advisory Board.** Establish a board of 3-5 members including:
   - At least one person with qualitative research ethics expertise
   - At least one community representative (not affiliated with Beat)
   - At least one person with data protection expertise
   - Gender and cultural diversity in composition

2. **Partnership with a university REC.** Several UK universities offer ethical review services for external organisations. This provides institutional credibility and established processes.

3. **Compliance with ESRC standards by policy.** Adopt the ESRC Framework as Beat's governing standard and document compliance at each stage. This is the minimum acceptable approach.

**For Mozilla compliance:** Mozilla's Trustworthy AI framework emphasises transparency, accountability, community governance, and data stewardship. An independent ethics advisory board with community representation directly addresses these requirements and demonstrates that Beat is not self-certifying its own ethics.

### 5.3 Mozilla Trustworthy AI Alignment

Mozilla's framework for trustworthy AI focuses on:

- **Transparency** -- research methods, data handling, and analytical processes must be documented and auditable
- **Accountability** -- clear lines of responsibility for ethical decisions
- **Community governance** -- affected communities should have voice in how research about them is conducted
- **Data stewardship** -- treating data as a responsibility, not an asset

Mozilla's Data Futures Lab specifically incubates "products and platforms radically redesigning what trustworthy data stewardship looks like." Beat's ethical framework should be designed with this standard in mind -- not as a compliance checkbox, but as a core feature.

## 6. Informed Consent: Beyond Consent Theatre

### 6.1 The Problem with Standard Consent

Standard informed consent practice -- handing someone a form, asking them to sign it, and proceeding -- is what critics call "consent theatre." It is:
- Legally defensive but ethically hollow
- Incomprehensible to many participants (consent forms are typically written at a reading level far above the general population)
- A one-time event that fails to account for the evolving nature of qualitative interviews
- Predicated on the fiction that participants can meaningfully consent to disclosures they have not yet made

Research confirms: common assessment questions like "Do you understand?" are unhelpful because participants may not know what questions to ask about what they do not understand.

### 6.2 Continuous Consent Model

Beat adopts a **continuous consent** (also called "process consent") model, as articulated by Klykken (2022) and others:

**Pre-interview:**
- Verbal explanation of the research purpose in plain language (no jargon, no acronyms)
- Written information sheet (maximum one A4 page, reading age 12 or below)
- Explanation of what will happen with their words -- who will read them, how they will be used, where they might appear
- Explicit statement that they can stop at any time, skip any question, and withdraw after the interview
- Signed or recorded verbal consent (verbal consent is valid under GDPR and may be more appropriate for participants with literacy barriers)
- Time gap between information and consent where possible -- do not hand someone a form and immediately ask them to sign it

**During interview:**
- Check in at transitions: "I'd like to ask about something more personal -- is that okay?"
- If a participant discloses something sensitive or becomes emotional: "Would you like to take a break? We can stop here if you prefer."
- If a participant shares something that could identify them: "That's really helpful. Just so you know, I would anonymise [detail] before including it anywhere. Is that okay?"

**Post-interview:**
- Offer to review the transcript or summary
- Reconfirm consent for specific quotes to be used
- Provide a clear mechanism for withdrawal (email, phone, or in person)
- Specify a withdrawal deadline (e.g., 30 days after interview) after which anonymised data may already be incorporated into analysis

### 6.3 Consent for Specific Data Types

| Data Type | Consent Requirement |
|---|---|
| Audio recording | Explicit opt-in before recording begins |
| Verbatim quotes in publications | Separate consent, ideally after transcript review |
| Location data | Explain what is collected and at what precision |
| Demographic data | Explain why each item is asked; participant can decline any item |
| Follow-up contact | Separate consent with specified purpose and timeframe |
| Data sharing with partners | Named partners, specific purposes, separate consent |

### 6.4 Consent in Low-Literacy and Multilingual Contexts

- Verbal consent recorded on audio is valid and may be preferable
- Information sheets should be available in community languages
- Visual consent aids (pictorial information sheets) should be developed for contexts where literacy cannot be assumed
- When using interpreters for consent, the interpreter must be briefed on the consent process and must translate the full explanation, not a summary

## 7. Anonymisation and Re-Identification Prevention

### 7.1 The Re-Identification Problem

The combination of date of birth, gender, and postcode is sufficient to uniquely identify approximately 87% of the US population. The UK is similar. For civic field research, the combination of:
- Neighbourhood
- Approximate age
- Ethnicity
- A specific civic complaint or story

...is frequently sufficient to identify an individual, especially in small communities. This is the **mosaic effect** (also called jigsaw identification) -- individually safe data points combine to become identifying.

### 7.2 Anonymisation Techniques

**k-Anonymity:** Ensure that each participant's quasi-identifiers (age range, gender, neighbourhood) are shared by at least k-1 other participants in any published dataset. For Beat's purposes, k >= 5 is the minimum standard.

**l-Diversity:** Within each group of k-anonymous records, ensure there are at least l different values for sensitive attributes. This prevents inference attacks where all members of a k-anonymous group share the same sensitive characteristic.

**t-Closeness:** The distribution of sensitive attributes within any equivalence class should be close to the distribution in the full dataset. This prevents skewness attacks.

**Practical anonymisation protocol for Beat:**

1. **At transcription:**
   - Replace all names with pseudonyms
   - Replace specific addresses with neighbourhood-level descriptors
   - Replace employer names with sector descriptors ("a local care home" not "Sunrise Care Ltd")
   - Replace school names with generic descriptors
   - Generalise ages to decade ranges
   - Remove or generalise dates

2. **At analysis:**
   - Use different pseudonyms for the same participant across different published extracts (smoke-screening) to prevent jigsaw identification
   - Rephrase verbatim quotes where the specific wording is identifiable without losing meaning
   - Break links between data points that together could identify (e.g., do not publish "a Somali woman in her 40s in Easton who complained about [specific issue]" -- generalise at least one dimension)

3. **At publication:**
   - Review all quotes for indirect identifiers
   - Apply a "motivated intruder" test: could a neighbour, employer, or local official identify this person?
   - When in doubt, further generalise or suppress

### 7.3 Special Risks for Civic Research

Civic burden data carries unique re-identification risks:
- **Small-area research:** In a ward of 8,000 people, a demographic description plus a specific complaint can be highly identifying
- **Local knowledge:** Neighbours, councillors, and housing officers may recognise participants from stories even without names
- **Temporal correlation:** If interviews happen during a known controversy (e.g., a planning dispute), context alone can identify participants
- **Retaliation risk:** Participants who describe problems with landlords, employers, or councils may face consequences if identified

**Mitigation:** Where retaliation risk is identified, consider whether the data should be collected at all, and if so, whether it should be analysed but never published in any form that could be traced.

## 8. Sampling and Representativeness

### 8.1 The Bias Problem in Field Research

Beat researchers operate in the field -- on streets, in community centres, at bus stops, outside schools. This is inherently **convenience sampling**, which is the sampling method most susceptible to bias. People who are visible, available, and willing to talk to a stranger are systematically different from those who are not.

Groups likely to be underrepresented in convenience sampling:
- People working multiple jobs (not available during daytime)
- People with caring responsibilities (constrained mobility)
- People with social anxiety or distrust of institutions
- Undocumented residents (fear of authority figures)
- People with language barriers
- Elderly people with mobility limitations
- Young people (may not be in spaces researchers frequent)
- Night-shift workers

### 8.2 Bias-Aware Sampling Strategies

**Stratified purposive sampling:** Before entering the field, define the demographic composition of the target area using census data and local authority statistics. Set quotas for age, gender, ethnicity, and tenure type (homeowner/renter/social housing). Track recruitment against quotas and adjust sampling locations and times accordingly.

**Time stratification:** Conduct interviews at different times of day and different days of the week to reach different populations. Early morning (commuters), midday (retired people, parents), evening (workers), weekends (families).

**Location stratification:** Do not interview only in one type of space. Vary between:
- Community centres (older residents, community-engaged residents)
- School gates (parents/carers)
- High streets and markets (diverse, transient)
- Places of worship (specific communities -- with permission and cultural sensitivity)
- Libraries (varied demographics)
- GP surgery waiting rooms (with NHS permission)
- Public transport stops (commuters, lower-income residents)

**Snowball sampling with controls:** Snowball sampling (asking participants to refer others) can access hard-to-reach populations but introduces homophily bias -- people refer people like themselves. Mitigate by:
- Using multiple independent "seeds" (starting points) from different communities
- Setting a maximum chain length (e.g., no more than 3 referrals from any single seed)
- Tracking the referral network and identifying gaps
- Never relying on snowball sampling alone

### 8.3 Representativeness Reporting

Every dataset published by Beat must include a sampling report documenting:
- Target area demographics (from census / local authority data)
- Achieved sample demographics
- Identified gaps and known biases
- Sampling locations, times, and methods used
- Refusal rate (proportion of people approached who declined)

This is not optional. Publishing civic burden data without representativeness context is irresponsible -- it risks mistaking the burdens of the accessible for the burdens of the area.

## 9. Cultural Sensitivity

### 9.1 Interviewing Across Language Barriers

**Using interpreters:**
- The interpreter is not a neutral conduit. They are a cultural broker who shapes the interaction. Acknowledge and plan for this.
- Brief interpreters thoroughly before the interview: explain the research purpose, the consent process, the style of questioning (open-ended, non-leading), and the importance of translating fully rather than summarising.
- When possible, use community interpreters who share the participant's cultural background -- but be aware this can inhibit disclosure if the community is small and people know each other.
- After the interview, debrief with the interpreter about cultural nuances that may not have translated.
- Consider the interpreter's own wellbeing if traumatic content is disclosed.

**Multilingual materials:**
- Information sheets and consent forms must be available in the main community languages of the research area
- Identify languages from local authority demographic data
- Use professional translation, not machine translation, for consent materials
- Back-translate to check accuracy

### 9.2 Interviewing in Religious Spaces

- **Seek permission** from religious leaders before approaching congregants. Explain the research purpose and obtain agreement.
- **Respect the space.** Do not interview during services, prayer times, or rituals. Use adjacent community rooms where available.
- **Gender sensitivity.** In some faith communities, participants may prefer a researcher of the same gender. Accommodate this where possible.
- **Fasting periods.** Be aware of Ramadan, Lent, and other fasting periods. Offering food during fasting is disrespectful. Scheduling long interviews during fasting periods may be burdensome.
- **Recording.** Some participants may be uncomfortable with audio recording in or near a religious space. Be prepared to take notes instead.

### 9.3 Power Dynamics

- **Researcher positionality.** The researcher's own identity (race, gender, class, accent, clothing) affects the interaction. A researcher who looks or sounds like "authority" may get different responses than one who does not. Acknowledge this in analysis.
- **Institutional distrust.** Many communities -- particularly those with experience of immigration enforcement, police over-policing, or hostile welfare systems -- have legitimate reasons to distrust researchers. Do not take distrust personally. Build trust through repeated presence, community partnerships, and transparent communication about how data will be used.
- **Gatekeepers.** Community leaders who facilitate access also shape access. They may steer researchers toward "respectable" participants and away from dissenting voices. Use multiple entry points.

## 10. Trauma-Informed Interviewing

### 10.1 Why This Matters for Civic Research

Civic burden research will surface trauma. Residents describing interactions with housing services may disclose domestic violence. Residents describing immigration processes may describe detention. Residents describing poverty may describe going without food. This is not hypothetical -- it is the nature of asking people about what weighs on them.

Research evidence indicates that survivors of trauma can participate in research about their experiences without experiencing unexpected or high levels of distress, and that many individuals find it beneficial to speak about their experience with a neutral listener. However, this requires proper protocols.

### 10.2 Core Principles

1. **Safety.** The physical and emotional safety of the participant is paramount. Interviews should take place in settings where the participant feels safe. If a participant becomes distressed, the interview stops. No data point is worth harm.

2. **Trustworthiness and transparency.** Be honest about who you are, what you are doing, and what will happen with the data. Do not make promises you cannot keep (e.g., "I'll make sure the council does something about this").

3. **Peer support.** Where possible, have support available -- either a co-researcher who can step in, or a list of local support services to offer.

4. **Collaboration and mutuality.** The participant controls the pace and direction of the conversation. They decide what to share and what to withhold.

5. **Empowerment, voice, and choice.** The participant's agency must be maintained throughout. They choose whether to continue, what to discuss, and whether their words are used.

### 10.3 Distress Protocol

**If a participant becomes visibly distressed:**
1. Pause. Do not continue asking questions.
2. Acknowledge: "I can see this is difficult. Thank you for sharing that."
3. Offer options: "Would you like to take a break, change the subject, or stop the interview?"
4. Respect their choice. If they want to continue, follow their lead.
5. Do not probe further into the distressing topic unless the participant leads there.
6. At the end of the interview, check in: "How are you feeling? Is there anything I can do?"
7. Offer the local support resource card (pre-prepared with local service numbers -- Samaritans, domestic violence helpline, Citizens Advice, local mental health services).

**If a participant discloses abuse, violence, or criminal activity:**
1. Listen. Do not express shock, judgement, or disbelief.
2. Thank them for trusting you with that.
3. Do not promise confidentiality you cannot guarantee -- if a child or vulnerable adult is at immediate risk, you may have a duty to report.
4. Know the local safeguarding referral pathway before entering the field.
5. Offer the relevant support service number.
6. Document the disclosure separately from the interview transcript, following the safeguarding protocol.

**If a participant discloses suicidal ideation:**
1. Take it seriously. Do not minimise.
2. Ask directly: "Are you having thoughts of harming yourself?"
3. If yes and the risk appears imminent, follow the emergency protocol (call emergency services with the participant's knowledge where possible).
4. If the risk is not imminent, provide the Samaritans number (116 123) and encourage them to reach out.
5. Follow up within 24 hours if they have consented to contact.

### 10.4 Researcher Wellbeing

Researchers conducting civic burden interviews will experience **vicarious trauma** (also called secondary traumatic stress). This is not a sign of weakness -- it is a predictable consequence of sustained empathic engagement with distressing material.

**Mandatory provisions:**
- Debrief after every interview session (with a peer, supervisor, or support professional)
- Maximum of 3 in-depth interviews per day
- Regular supervision (fortnightly minimum) with someone trained in supporting qualitative researchers
- Access to counselling or employee assistance programme
- Permission to take breaks from fieldwork without stigma
- Training in recognising signs of vicarious trauma (sleep disruption, emotional numbing, irritability, intrusive thoughts)

## 11. Data Lifecycle and Retention

### 11.1 Collection

- Audio recordings encrypted at rest on the recording device
- Field notes kept in a locked notebook or encrypted digital device
- No participant names recorded in field notes -- use codes assigned at consent
- Contact details (for follow-up) stored separately from interview data

### 11.2 Transcription

- Transcription by trained, vetted personnel (or the researcher themselves)
- If using external transcription services, a Data Processing Agreement (DPA) is required under GDPR
- AI transcription tools: conduct a DPIA before use; ensure no data is retained by the provider; check server locations (must be adequate jurisdiction)
- Anonymisation occurs at transcription stage

### 11.3 Storage

- All data stored in encrypted, access-controlled systems
- Access limited to named researchers
- No interview data on personal devices without encryption
- No interview data in personal email or consumer cloud storage (Google Drive, Dropbox) without organisational controls

### 11.4 Retention and Deletion

- Audio recordings: delete within 30 days of verified transcription
- Contact details: delete within 30 days of final follow-up
- Anonymised transcripts: retain for the duration of the research programme plus 3 years (for verification and replication)
- Consent records: retain for 6 years after the end of the research programme (legal compliance)
- Published datasets: retain indefinitely in anonymised form

### 11.5 Breach Protocol

If a data breach occurs (loss of device, unauthorised access, accidental disclosure):
1. Report to the Data Protection Officer / responsible person within 24 hours
2. Assess the risk to data subjects
3. If the breach is likely to result in a risk to individuals' rights and freedoms, notify the ICO within 72 hours
4. If the breach is likely to result in a high risk to individuals, notify affected participants without undue delay
5. Document the breach, its effects, and remedial action

## 12. Quality Assurance and Audit

### 12.1 Interview Quality

- Audio-record all interviews (with consent) for quality review
- Supervisory review of at least 20% of transcripts per researcher
- Peer review of coding and thematic analysis
- Inter-coder reliability checks when multiple researchers are coding

### 12.2 Ethical Compliance Audit

- Quarterly review of consent records (are they complete? properly stored?)
- Annual review of anonymisation effectiveness (apply motivated intruder test to published data)
- Annual review of sampling representativeness across all fieldwork areas
- Ethics advisory board review of any new research areas or methods

### 12.3 Participant Feedback

- Offer all participants the opportunity to review how their contributions were used
- Track and respond to complaints
- Report complaints and their resolution to the ethics advisory board
