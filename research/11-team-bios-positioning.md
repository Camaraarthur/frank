# Team Bios & Positioning — Mozilla Democracy x AI Grant

## Arthur Camara

Arthur Camara is a designer-technologist and recent graduate of the Bartlett School of Architecture at UCL, where his final project explored direct democracy and participatory governance. He has published research on agent-based conversation analysis (AHFE 2024) and co-founded an AI platform that uses LLM agent chains to process and visualise large volumes of conversation data. ### Long Bio (paragraph)
Arthur Camara is a designer and technologist whose work sits at the intersection of AI, human conversation, and democratic participation. A recent graduate of the Bartlett School of Architecture at UCL, his final university project focused on direct democracy — investigating how design and technology can reshape how communities make decisions together. Beyond academia, Arthur co-founded Insights (2023), a platform that uses LLM agent chains to analyse recordings and digital footprints, enabling users to search, revisit, and extract meaning from their conversations. This work led to a peer-reviewed publication at the Conference on Applied Human Factors and Ergonomics (AHFE 2024) on agent-based conversation analysis and visualisation. He has also worked at Carlo Ratti Associati on strategy and innovation projects, and has experience building AI tools in professional contexts, including leading a partnership with Google to develop an internal AI agent for an architectural practice. Arthur is the technical lead for this project, responsible for designing and building the platform that will facilitate structured democratic deliberation using AI.

## Team Composition Analysis: Why This Pairing Works

### The Complementary Expertise Narrative

This team combines two distinct but deeply complementary skill sets that together cover exactly what a democracy-x-AI project requires: the ability to **build the technology** and the ability to **design the democratic process it serves**.

### What Arthur brings that Mikhail does not:
- **Technical platform development**: Arthur can design, build, and ship the AI-powered deliberation tools. He is not a theorist who needs to hire developers — he is the developer.
- **AI/ML applied experience**: Published research on LLM agent chains for conversation analysis, plus hands-on experience with vector embeddings, prompt engineering, and building user-facing AI products.
- **Conversation-as-data expertise**: His work on Insights directly addresses the core technical challenge of this project — how do you take messy, unstructured human conversation and extract structured, meaningful democratic signal from it?
- **Design thinking**: Trained at the Bartlett (one of the world's leading design schools), Arthur approaches technology not as an engineering problem but as a human experience problem. This matters for democratic tools, which fail when they are technically correct but unusable.

### What Mikhail brings that Arthur does not:
- **Institutional governance knowledge**: Mikhail has worked inside the UN system. He understands how governance programmes are structured, evaluated, and legitimised — not from textbooks, but from coordinating them.
- **Policy research methodology**: His training in politics and international relations provides the analytical frameworks needed to assess whether a democratic AI tool is actually producing better deliberation, not just more efficient conversation.
- **Stakeholder and civil society engagement**: At UNOCT, Mikhail worked with NGOs and member states across different political contexts. He understands the diplomatic and practical challenges of getting diverse groups to participate in structured processes.
- **Counter-terrorism and securitisation lens**: While this might seem tangential, securitisation theory is fundamentally about how issues get framed and who gets to participate in defining threats and responses — directly relevant to democratic AI, where the framing of questions and inclusion of voices is everything.
- **International and cross-cultural perspective**: Experience working across MENA and Central Asia means Mikhail understands that "democracy" does not look the same everywhere, and that tools must be designed with cultural and political plurality in mind.

### The Strategic Strength

The strongest aspect of this team for a Mozilla Democracy x AI grant is that **neither member is a pure academic**. Both have built or operated real systems:
- Arthur has built AI products and published on them.
- Mikhail has coordinated international governance programmes.

This means the project is not a research proposal that might one day become a tool. It is a tool being built by someone who can build it, guided by someone who understands the governance context it needs to serve.

Additionally, both team members are **UCL graduates**, which provides a shared institutional foundation and network, while their disciplines (architecture/technology and politics/international relations) represent exactly the kind of cross-faculty collaboration that funders value.

### The Honest Gap

The team is early-career. Neither member has a decade of academic publications or a track record of managing large grants. This is worth acknowledging upfront in the application — but it can be positioned as a strength: this is a team unburdened by institutional inertia, building with urgency, and motivated by genuine conviction rather than career incentives. Mozilla, as an organisation that funds builders and activists rather than established academics, is well-positioned to appreciate this.
