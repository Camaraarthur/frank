# International Precedents for Structured Civic Listening & Community Intelligence

Research compiled March 2026. For each case: what they did, scale, outcomes, why it worked or failed, and what we can learn.

---

## 1. Taiwan's vTaiwan / Join Platform

**What they did:** Following the 2014 Sunflower Movement protests, Taiwan launched vTaiwan (2015), a civic participation platform powered by Pol.is. Audrey Tang, a civic hacker from the g0v movement, became Taiwan's first Digital Minister and championed the project. Pol.is works by having participants submit short statements and vote agree/disagree on others' statements -- critically, there is no reply function, which eliminates trolling and flame wars. The system uses machine learning to cluster participants into opinion groups and surfaces points of consensus across groups.

**Scale:** 200,000 people on the mailing list by 2020. 26 issues discussed between 2015-2018, with 80% leading to decisive government action. A separate government platform, Join, eventually attracted millions of users.

**Outcomes:** Successfully crowdsourced legislation on closely-held company law. Resolved a deadlocked dispute between Uber and taxi drivers by finding consensus positions. Addressed internet alcohol sales regulation and revenge porn laws. Taiwan became the least polarized nation in Asia and the most democratic according to the Democracy Index.

**Why it worked (initially):**
- Pol.is's design structurally prevents polarization -- no reply threads, visual clustering shows common ground
- Government commitment to act on results gave the process legitimacy
- Emerged from genuine civic energy (Sunflower Movement), not top-down mandate
- Audrey Tang as a bridge between civic tech community and government

**Why it stalled:**
- Government never mandated adoption of vTaiwan recommendations -- legislators didn't take it seriously
- Restricted to digital-space issues (online sales, tech regulation) rather than expanding to harder topics -- "tokenistic participation"
- Platform usability was poor -- four different platforms cobbled together confused users
- The government launched Join as a separate platform, making vTaiwan redundant
- When it tackled Uber vs. taxis, some taxi drivers felt excluded because they weren't comfortable with digital tools
- Member attrition after 2019, pandemic disruption, near-death experience
- Hasn't been used for any major decision since 2018

**What we can learn:** The Pol.is consensus-finding mechanism is genuinely innovative and works. But a tool without institutional mandate dies. The government cherry-picked easy issues they already knew how to handle. Technology that increases citizen voice without increasing government capacity to respond just "increased the gap between citizen voice and citizen response" (Tiago Peixoto). The 2023 relaunch focused on AI governance suggests the tool works best for specific, bounded policy questions rather than as a general governance platform.

## 2. Estonia's e-Democracy (Rahvaalgatus)

**What they did:** Estonia, already the world's most digitally advanced government (e-Residency, digital ID, e-voting), launched Rahvaalgatus.ee in 2016 as a citizen initiative platform. Citizens can draft proposals, collect digital signatures, and submit them directly to Parliament or local municipalities. A proposal to Parliament needs 1,000 signatures; local government proposals need signatures from 1% of eligible voters. Signature collection has an 18-month window.

**Scale:** Over 550 initiatives launched. 820,000+ signatures collected (over a third of Estonia's population -- the EU equivalent would be 200 million). 119 initiatives sent to Parliament, 60 to local municipalities. In 2023, the platform's digital signatures (313,868) actually exceeded parliamentary election digital votes (312,182).

**Outcomes:** Real legislative impact. A 2019 initiative to ban fur farms resulted in Parliament banning them (effective July 2023). A 2020 petition to amend family law led to legalization of same-sex marriage (June 2023). Multiple other initiatives became law or influenced policy.

**Why it worked:**
- Built on top of existing world-class digital infrastructure (digital ID, e-signatures already universal)
- Clear, binding pathway: collect signatures -> Parliament MUST consider it
- Low friction -- citizens already use digital signatures for everything
- Manageable country size (1.3M people) means civic tech doesn't need to scale to hundreds of millions
- Platform run by an independent NGO (Citizen OS Foundation), not the government itself

**Why it's limited:**
- Works because Estonia is tiny, highly educated, and already fully digitized -- hard to replicate
- Parliament must consider initiatives but isn't bound to pass them
- Platform handles petition/initiative flow but doesn't do deliberation or consensus-building

**What we can learn:** The binding pathway matters enormously -- "Parliament must consider" creates real stakes. Building on existing digital infrastructure rather than creating parallel systems reduces friction. The independent NGO governance model (not government-run, not private company) is worth studying. But this is fundamentally a petition system, not a deliberation or intelligence-gathering system.

## 3. Iceland's Crowdsourced Constitution

**What they did:** After the 2008 financial collapse destroyed public trust in institutions, Iceland attempted to crowdsource a new constitution. In November 2010, a National Forum of 950 randomly selected citizens identified core values. A 25-member elected Constitutional Council then drafted a new constitution over four months, soliciting public feedback through social media. Citizens could comment on drafts in real-time. The final document was put to a national referendum in October 2012.

**Scale:** 950 citizens in the National Forum. 25 elected council members. Thousands of public comments through social media. National referendum with two-thirds majority approval (67% voted yes).

**Outcomes:** The constitution was approved by the public but never implemented. Parliament blocked the bill in spring 2013. Despite winning the referendum, the crowdsourced constitution died a political death. Iceland still uses its old constitution.

**Why it succeeded methodologically:**
- Random selection for the National Forum ensured genuine representativeness
- Open, transparent process with real-time public input built trust
- The referendum showed genuine public support (67%)
- Demonstrated that ordinary citizens can engage with complex constitutional questions

**Why it failed politically:**
- No clear institutional pathway: when the Constitutional Council finished, it was unclear what would happen next
- Parliament had no obligation to adopt the result -- and didn't
- The Council of Europe's Venice Commission criticized the draft's quality -- lack of consistency, complex institutional design
- The Council didn't have enough time, tools, or training to properly process crowd input or provide feedback
- Political parties saw it as threatening their power and had incentives to block
- Elections changed the government composition -- the new government didn't own the process
- Legal scholars attacked the draft's technical quality

**What we can learn:** This is the canonical case of "methodological success, political failure." Public enthusiasm and even a referendum win mean nothing without an institutional commitment to implement. The process needed to be embedded in the political system from the start, not created as an external experiment that Parliament could simply ignore. Also: you can't crowdsource a constitution in four months. The timeline was absurdly compressed for the complexity of the task. And expert review matters -- citizen input and technical legal quality are both necessary.

## 4. Ireland's Citizens' Assembly

**What they did:** Ireland convened two major deliberative bodies. The Convention on the Constitution (2012-2014) brought together 66 randomly selected citizens and 33 politicians. The Citizens' Assembly (2016-2018) comprised 99 randomly selected citizens plus an appointed chairperson. Both addressed deeply divisive issues the political system had been unable to resolve for decades. The process was carefully structured: members received information from experts, heard testimony, deliberated in small groups with trained facilitators, and voted on recommendations.

**Scale:** 99 citizens in the Assembly. Five weekend sessions over six months for the abortion question alone. 25 expert presentations heard. 12,000 public submissions received (300 reviewed in detail). The subsequent referendum on abortion saw 64.1% vote to repeal the Eighth Amendment -- almost exactly matching the Assembly's 64% recommendation.

**Outcomes:** Transformative. The Convention recommended a referendum on marriage equality -- Ireland voted yes in 2015, becoming the first country to legalize same-sex marriage by popular vote. The Citizens' Assembly recommended repealing the constitutional ban on abortion -- Ireland voted to repeal in 2018, ending decades of political deadlock. Both were issues that politicians had been terrified to touch.

**Why it worked:**
- Randomly selected citizens provided political cover -- politicians could say "the people recommended this"
- Structured deliberation with expert input meant decisions were informed, not reactive
- Trained facilitators ensured equality of voice -- no one dominated
- Time was adequate -- months of weekends, not a rushed process
- Clear institutional pathway: Assembly recommends -> Parliament considers -> referendum
- The Assembly's composition was broadly representative (age, gender, class, region)
- Information provision was balanced and fact-based, not advocacy-driven
- Small group deliberation (roundtables) combined with plenary sessions

**Limitations:**
- Expensive and slow -- not suitable for routine governance
- Works for big, binary, constitutional questions; less clear for complex policy tradeoffs
- Requires genuine political will to establish and follow through
- 99 people is not "the public" -- legitimacy rests on the random selection process

**What we can learn:** This is the gold standard for structured civic deliberation on divisive issues. The key insight: when citizens are given time, information, and facilitation, they can handle the hardest questions -- and their conclusions predict the broader public's views with remarkable accuracy (64% vs 66%). The Assembly worked because it had a clear mandate, adequate time, expert input, and a binding pathway to action. The "Irish Model" is now being replicated worldwide. For civic listening tools: the combination of expert information + facilitated small-group deliberation + representative sampling is the formula that works.

## 5. Barcelona's Decidim

**What they did:** Barcelona developed Decidim ("We Decide") as an open-source digital democracy platform, launched in 2016 under Mayor Ada Colau's progressive government. It handles participatory budgeting, citizen proposals, collaborative legislation drafting, and civic consultations. The platform is free, open-source software that any city or organization can adopt and modify.

**Scale:** 80,500 registered users on Decidim.Barcelona. 64,571 people participated in the participatory budgeting process since 2020. The 2016 Municipal Action Plan generated 9,000+ citizen proposals, 400 face-to-face meetings, and 150,000+ support votes. Citizens allocated up to 75 million euros (5% of municipal budget) between 2020-2023. Globally, Decidim is now used by 450+ organizations in 30+ countries.

**Outcomes:** Citizens selected 76 out of 184 projects for implementation (30 million euros). 822 citizen proposals were submitted and prioritized. The platform brought 80+ local and supra-local governments into a collaborative ecosystem. Decidim became the most widely adopted open-source participatory democracy platform in the world.

**Why it worked:**
- Open source created trust -- citizens can audit the code, no hidden algorithms
- Community governance model -- not controlled by one city or vendor
- Combined digital and in-person participation (400 face-to-face meetings alongside online)
- Real money at stake (75M euros) gave participation meaning
- Political leadership committed to the model (Colau government)
- Built as infrastructure, not a one-off project -- designed for sustainability

**Limitations and challenges:**
- Inclusion gap: digital participation still skews urban, educated, male
- Language barriers in multilingual contexts (Barcelona has 800+ languages spoken)
- Requires sustained political commitment -- platform alone doesn't create participation
- Participatory budgeting typically controls only 5% of the budget -- symbolic vs. transformative?
- Risk of "participation fatigue" when too many processes compete for attention

**What we can learn:** Open source matters for civic infrastructure -- it builds trust and creates a global commons. The combination of digital tools with in-person facilitation is essential for inclusion. Real budgetary power (not just advisory) drives engagement. But 5% of the budget is a tension: enough to attract participation, not enough to feel transformative. The global adoption of Decidim shows the value of building reusable civic infrastructure rather than bespoke solutions. Design for the ecosystem, not just one use case.

## 6. Porto Alegre Participatory Budgeting

**What they did:** Porto Alegre, Brazil pioneered participatory budgeting (PB) in 1989 under the Workers' Party (PT). Citizens attended neighborhood assemblies to identify priorities, elected delegates to regional forums, and those delegates negotiated budget allocations with the city government. The process was designed specifically to include the urban poor, who had been systematically excluded from politics.

**Scale:** Participation grew from ~1,000 people in 1990 to 40,000 in 1999. The model has since been replicated by 2,700+ governments worldwide. Porto Alegre ran PB continuously from 1989 to 2017 -- nearly 30 years of data.

**Outcomes:** Dramatic transformation of public services for the poor. Sewer and water connections went from 75% to 98% of households (1988-1997). Public housing units went from sheltering 1,700 to 27,000 people. Number of schools quadrupled. Health and education spending rose from 13% to 40% of the budget. Most significantly, PB reversed typical political participation patterns -- the poor participated at higher rates than the middle class, the opposite of normal politics.

**Why it worked:**
- Physical assemblies in neighborhoods meant no digital divide barrier
- Directly controlled real infrastructure spending that visibly improved lives
- The poor could see concrete results in their neighborhoods -- sewers, schools, housing
- Created a feedback loop: participate -> see results -> participate more
- Strong political party (PT) committed to the model for four consecutive terms
- Democratic legitimacy: delegates were elected, not appointed

**Why it declined:**
- After four PT terms, an opposition government took over and deprioritized PB
- Federal funding in the 2000s came without participation requirements, undercutting the model
- The PT itself shifted focus as it gained national power
- PB was suspended in Porto Alegre in 2017 -- the birthplace abandoned its own invention
- Never institutionalized in law -- remained dependent on political will
- As the city became more complex, the assembly model became harder to scale

**What we can learn:** This is the most important precedent in the field. Thirty years of data show that participatory budgeting genuinely transforms services for the poor AND reverses political exclusion patterns. The physical assembly model was key to inclusion. But Porto Alegre's decline proves the fatal weakness: without legal institutionalization, everything depends on continued political will. When the political party that championed it lost power, PB died. The lesson is stark -- civic participation systems must be embedded in law, not just in political programs. Also: visible, concrete results (sewers, schools) drive continued engagement in a way that abstract "having your voice heard" does not.

## 7. France's Grand Debat National

**What they did:** In response to the Yellow Vest (Gilets Jaunes) protests of late 2018, President Macron launched the Grand Debat National in January 2019. Citizens were invited to participate through an online platform, local town hall meetings, and municipal grievance registries (cahiers de doleances, echoing the 1789 Revolution). The consultation covered four themes: taxation and public spending, state organization and public services, ecological transition, and democracy and citizenship.

**Scale:** Massive: 1.9 million online contributions. 10,134 local meetings. 16,337 municipalities opened grievance registries. 2.8 million visitors to the platform. ~500,000 distinct contributors. 27,374 letters and emails received. By raw numbers, one of the largest consultation exercises in democratic history.

**Outcomes:** Virtually nothing. As one analysis put it: "Nothing has been done with the information gathered to justify the expense and the effort." No radical policy changes resulted. Nothing happened that wouldn't have happened anyway without the debate. Participants publicly expressed frustration. The exercise was soon overtaken by the Notre Dame fire (April 2019) and forgotten. The unaddressed grievances that drove the Yellow Vests -- cost of living, rural service collapse -- resurfaced in the 2024 elections, driving voters toward extremist parties.

**Why it failed:**
- No binding commitment to act on results -- pure consultation theater
- No follow-up mechanism was established before launch
- Words like "consultation," "participation," and "deliberation" were used interchangeably with no clarity about what citizens were actually deciding
- Government controlled the framing (four pre-set themes) rather than letting citizens define the agenda
- The Yellow Vests themselves saw it as illegitimate and launched a parallel "Vrai Debat" (Real Debate)
- Only 38% of voters believed the government would act on their input (IFOP poll)
- Analysis of the collected data was never properly completed
- Designed as crisis management, not as genuine democratic infrastructure
- One columnist called it "a magician's sleight of hand; a deliberate act of misdirection" -- pretending to listen

**What we can learn:** Scale without commitment is worse than nothing. 1.9 million contributions that are ignored create more cynicism than never asking. This is the clearest case of "consultation theater" -- participation as legitimacy prop rather than genuine power-sharing. The Grand Debat teaches us: never launch a listening exercise without a pre-committed, transparent mechanism for what happens with the input. If you ask people to spend time contributing and then ignore them, you actively damage democratic trust. Also: crisis-driven, top-down consultations lack legitimacy because citizens correctly perceive them as serving the leader's agenda, not theirs.

## 8. South Korea's Presidential Petition System

**What they did:** President Moon Jae-in launched the National Petition to the Blue House (Cheong Wa Dae) in August 2017, 100 days into his presidency. Any petition reaching 200,000 signatures within 30 days required an official government response. It was modeled partly on the Obama-era White House petitions platform but with a genuine commitment to respond.

**Scale:** 1.11 million petitions submitted over ~5 years. 516 million website visitors. 230 million total signatures. 286 petitions crossed the 200,000-signature threshold requiring an official response.

**Outcomes:** Mixed but real. The "Telegram Nth Room" digital sex crime scandal generated 9 petitions with 7.69 million combined signatures -- the government toughened punishments for digital sex crimes. A petition on retired detection dogs led Seoul National University to shut down an experimental program. Petitions on animal welfare, education policy, and criminal justice all received official responses and some led to policy changes.

**Why it worked (partially):**
- The 200,000-signature threshold created a clear, achievable bar
- Government commitment to respond within 30 days gave the system credibility
- Digital signatures were frictionless for South Korea's highly connected population
- The system amplified issues the media and politicians had been ignoring
- It created public pressure campaigns around specific, concrete issues

**Why it was limited/ended:**
- Platform was decommissioned in May 2022 when Moon left office
- The incoming Yoon administration replaced it with a different system (Gungminjean)
- Petitions could be performative -- responding to a petition is not the same as changing policy
- The system was tied to one president's political brand, not institutionalized
- Some petitions were frivolous or inflammatory, creating noise
- Government responses sometimes amounted to acknowledging the issue without committing to action

**What we can learn:** The forced-response threshold is a powerful mechanism -- it creates a clear social contract between citizens and government. But tying the system to a president's personal platform means it dies with the administration. The Korean case also shows the difference between "response" and "action" -- an official statement acknowledging an issue is not the same as policy change. Petition systems are good at surfacing salient issues but bad at handling complexity or tradeoffs. They channel emotion effectively but don't create deliberation.

## 9. India's MyGov Platform

**What they did:** Launched in July 2014 by the Modi government, MyGov is a citizen engagement platform allowing Indians to participate in discussions, complete tasks, vote in polls, and submit ideas for government programs. It spans topics from railway budgets to national holidays to the Prime Minister's monthly radio address (Mann Ki Baat).

**Scale:** 25+ million registered users by 2024. 3,000+ active discussions. 4 million+ comments across 822 discussion forums. 678,000+ task submissions across 885 tasks. India's scale makes this one of the largest civic engagement platforms in the world.

**Outcomes:** Some real integration into policy: citizen suggestions were incorporated into railway budgets (2015-2017). The Mann Ki Baat program regularly crowdsources topics from MyGov. The platform coordinates across governmental and non-governmental agencies -- rare in Indian bureaucracy. Some enrichment of policy quality through citizen input.

**Why it partially works:**
- Massive existing digital population to draw from
- Direct connection to the Prime Minister's office gives it status
- Cross-functional coordination across agencies is genuinely useful
- Vernacularization efforts (multiple Indian languages) expand reach

**Failures and limitations:**
- Digital divide: participation skews urban, educated, male despite India's diversity
- Engagement is heavily centralized -- the federal platform doesn't empower state or municipal governance
- Tension between political centralization and participatory federalism
- Platform functions more as a communication tool for the ruling party than a genuinely deliberative space
- No binding mechanism -- government can and does ignore input
- In a country of 1.4 billion, 25 million users represents less than 2% of the population
- Quality of engagement is unclear -- are people genuinely deliberating or just clicking?

**What we can learn:** Scale in raw numbers doesn't mean representative or deliberative engagement. 25 million sounds impressive but is 1.8% of India. The platform illustrates the "government communication tool" trap: when a civic platform is run by the executive and connected to the leader's personal brand, it becomes a channel for broadcasting, not listening. MyGov is useful infrastructure but it's not genuine civic deliberation. For any civic tool in a large, diverse country: vernacularization is essential but insufficient; you need to address structural power imbalances, not just language barriers.

## 10. Deliberative Polling (James Fishkin)

**What they did:** Stanford professor James Fishkin invented Deliberative Polling in 1988 and has since conducted 150+ experiments in 50+ countries. The method: take a scientific random sample of a population, give them balanced briefing materials, bring them together for facilitated small-group discussions with access to experts, then poll them again. The difference between pre- and post-deliberation opinions reveals what an informed public actually thinks.

**Scale:** 150+ experiments across the US, China, Mongolia, Japan, South Korea, Bulgaria, Brazil, Uganda, Northern Ireland, and many others. Sample sizes typically range from a few hundred to a thousand participants per poll.

**Key outcomes and findings:**
- ~70% of participants change their minds on at least some issues after deliberation
- Opinion shifts are statistically significant and tend toward moderation/centrism
- Changes persist for at least one year after the in-person gathering
- Participants become more informed (knowledge gains are measurable)
- Participants report increased feelings of political efficacy
- In Zeguo, China (pop. 120,000), deliberative polling determined the municipal budget for several years -- the government implemented the results
- In Northern Ireland, Protestant and Catholic parents deliberated school integration in Omagh and reached mutually agreeable conclusions on a deeply divisive issue
- In Bulgaria, deliberation on Roma integration shifted attitudes measurably

**Why it works:**
- Random sampling ensures representativeness -- not self-selected activists
- Balanced briefing materials counter misinformation and ignorance
- Small-group facilitation ensures everyone speaks, not just the loudest
- Access to experts means participants can ask clarifying questions
- The before/after measurement proves deliberation changes minds -- it's not just talk
- Works across cultures, political systems, and on deeply divisive issues

**Limitations:**
- Expensive and logistically complex -- bringing hundreds of randomly selected people together physically
- Doesn't scale to routine governance -- it's an event, not a system
- Results are advisory -- implementation depends on political will
- The "Deliberative Poll" trademark means it's controlled by one academic center
- A few hundred people deliberating doesn't equal democratic legitimacy to some critics
- Works best for specific policy questions, less clear for broad agenda-setting

**What we can learn:** Fishkin's work provides the strongest empirical evidence that deliberation works. When people are given information AND time to discuss, they become more informed, more moderate, and more engaged -- and these effects last. The 70% opinion-change rate demolishes the assumption that people's views are fixed. The key design elements: random sampling (not self-selection), balanced information (not advocacy), facilitated discussion (not debate), and expert access (not just opinion sharing). Any serious civic listening tool should incorporate these principles. The challenge is scaling them beyond expensive one-off events.

## 11. Community Organizing Traditions

### Saul Alinsky and Rules for Radicals

**What he did:** Alinsky (1909-1972) created the modern practice of community organizing, starting in Chicago's Back of the Yards neighborhood in the 1930s. His method: organizers enter a community, conduct "one-on-one" relational meetings to discover people's self-interest, identify shared grievances, build organizations around those grievances, and confront power holders through direct action. Published "Rules for Radicals" (1971) as a practical guide.

**Core principles:**
- Start by listening -- empathic listening to hopes, fears, dreams, specific concerns
- Build relationships before building organizations
- Organize around self-interest, not ideology
- Power concedes nothing without demand
- Local people must lead -- organizers facilitate, they don't dictate
- "Native leadership" -- develop leaders from within the community
- Confrontational when necessary -- comfort the afflicted, afflict the comfortable

**Legacy and influence:** Trained generations of organizers including those who trained Barack Obama. The Gamaliel Foundation, Industrial Areas Foundation (IAF), and PICO National Network all descend from Alinsky's methods. The methodology has been used in labor organizing, housing rights, education reform, immigration advocacy, and more.

### Obama-Era Community Organizing

**What they did:** Obama, trained as an Alinsky-style organizer in Chicago (Gamaliel network), brought organizing methodology into presidential campaigning. His 2008 campaign trained 3,000+ full-time organizers, built 1,100+ local leadership teams in Ohio alone, and engaged 2.2 million volunteers in structured activity.

**Key innovations:**
- "Public narrative" methodology (developed by Harvard's Marshall Ganz, himself a former Cesar Chavez organizer): Story of Self, Story of Us, Story of Now -- personal storytelling as a way to build shared purpose
- Structured volunteer teams: leader, data manager, phone bank coordinator, campus coordinator, volunteer coordinator
- Volunteers were taught to share their stories, listen, discuss problems, and develop their own plans -- not just follow scripts
- Combined digital tools with face-to-face organizing -- technology served relationships, not the other way around

**How it maps to civic listening:**
- The one-on-one meeting IS civic listening at its most basic -- sitting with someone, hearing their story, finding shared ground
- The emphasis on self-interest (not abstract "civic duty") is key to sustained engagement
- Organization around concrete issues that affect daily life drives participation
- Power analysis is essential -- who benefits, who decides, who is affected
- Leadership development from within communities creates sustainability
- Storytelling creates emotional connection and shared identity that data alone cannot

**What we can learn:** Community organizing is the offline ancestor of what civic tech tries to do digitally. The core lessons: listen first, organize around self-interest, build relationships before asking for action, develop local leaders, and always connect to concrete issues. The Obama campaign showed these principles can scale with technology -- but technology served the relationships, not the other way around. Most civic tech fails because it starts with the technology and hopes relationships will follow. Alinsky started with relationships and used whatever tools were available. Any civic intelligence system needs to be designed around relationship-building and concrete self-interest, not around "participation" as an abstract good.

## 12. Failure Cases -- Why Civic Tech Platforms Die

### The Civic Tech Graveyard

The Civic Tech Field Guide maintains a "graveyard" of ~70 defunct projects. Of the thousands of civic tech organizations worldwide, only about 70 core groups have lasted more than ten years without shutting down or being acquired.

### Specific failures:
- **Citizinvestor** -- civic crowdfunding platform, founders documented what went wrong
- **Carrotmob** -- collective action hub, shut down
- **Pledgebank** (mySociety) -- closed despite project vibrancy because other platforms (Groupon) provided overlapping services
- **TED.com's action platform** -- received a $985,000 Knight Foundation grant, failed to launch as intended
- **Grassroot** (South Africa) -- civic tech platform that couldn't sustain
- **Multiple African civic tech projects** -- Neema Iyer documented widespread failure across the continent
- **Various "social networks for political information"** -- Micah Sifry and Matt Stempeck found these are "likely to fail" as a category

### Structural reasons platforms die:

**1. No sustainable business model.** Civic tech sits in a market failure zone -- citizens won't pay for it, governments are slow purchasers, and advertisers aren't interested. Most survive on grant funding, which distorts priorities toward donor interests rather than community needs.

**2. The engagement cliff.** Initial excitement generates signups, media attention, and conference presentations. But sustained engagement requires ongoing value delivery. Most platforms see usage crater after the first few months. "Without broad, enduring civic participation, promising civic tech pilots frequently fade in adoption after initial buzz or donor funding dissipates."

**3. Government non-responsiveness.** "Well-intentioned city governments establish tools that never gain user attention, tools that obtain attention from select communities but fail to attract a broad user base over time, and tools that do little more than create a theater of civic engagement without actually increasing government responsiveness." If participation doesn't lead to visible results, people stop participating.

**4. Building for the wrong user.** Only a minority of civic tech organizations do user research before choosing a tool. Even fewer test with potential users. Organizations build what technologists think is cool, not what communities actually need.

**5. Competing with social media.** Civic platforms can't compete with Facebook, WhatsApp, or WeChat for attention. People are already on those platforms discussing civic issues -- asking them to go to a separate, less polished platform is a losing proposition.

**6. Digital divide and exclusion.** Platforms designed by and for urban, educated, tech-comfortable users systematically exclude the people most affected by civic decisions -- the poor, elderly, rural, and digitally disconnected.

**7. Technology-first, relationships-last.** The Alinsky tradition starts with listening and relationships. Most civic tech starts with code and features. This inversion is fatal.

**8. Political dependence.** Platforms championed by one political leader or party die when that leader leaves office (South Korea, Porto Alegre). Without legal institutionalization, civic tech is always one election away from extinction.

**9. Scope mismatch.** Some platforms try to be everything (general civic engagement) and end up being nothing. Others are too narrow to sustain engagement. Finding the right scope is critical and most get it wrong.

**10. The "fundraising success =/= project success" trap.** Grant-funded projects optimize for what funders want to see (impressive numbers, media coverage, conference appearances) rather than what creates durable civic infrastructure.

## Cross-Cutting Lessons

### What works:

1. **Binding pathways.** Ireland's Citizens' Assembly -> referendum pipeline. Estonia's Parliament-must-consider requirement. South Korea's forced-response threshold. When participation is connected to mandatory government action, it works. When it's advisory, it dies.

2. **Structured deliberation over raw input.** Fishkin's deliberative polls, Ireland's facilitated assemblies, and Pol.is's consensus-clustering all outperform petition systems, comment boxes, and open forums. Structure produces wisdom; unstructured input produces noise.

3. **Random selection over self-selection.** Every successful deliberative process uses random (sortition-based) sampling. Self-selected participants are unrepresentative by definition -- they skew toward the already-engaged, the angry, and the privileged.

4. **Concrete stakes.** Porto Alegre's budgets, Barcelona's 75M euros, real legislation in Estonia. Abstract "having your voice heard" doesn't sustain engagement. Real money, real laws, real infrastructure does.

5. **Hybrid digital-physical.** Every successful case combines online tools with in-person deliberation. Pure digital excludes the digitally disconnected. Pure physical doesn't scale. The blend is essential.

6. **Independent governance.** Estonia's NGO model, Decidim's open-source community governance, Ireland's independent chair. Government-run platforms become propaganda tools. Private-run platforms become products. Independent civic infrastructure is the sweet spot.

7. **Time and information.** Fishkin's 70% opinion-change rate happens because people get briefing materials AND facilitated discussion time. Quick polls and instant reactions produce garbage. Deliberation requires investment.

### What kills civic participation systems:

1. **Consultation theater.** The Grand Debat is the archetype. Asking without committing to act creates more cynicism than never asking at all.

2. **Political dependency.** Porto Alegre, South Korea, vTaiwan -- all declined or died when political champions left. Legal institutionalization is the only antidote.

3. **Technology-first design.** Building a platform and hoping people come. The graveyard is full of beautifully coded, perfectly designed platforms that no one used.

4. **Ignoring power dynamics.** Who gets to frame the questions? Who decides which input matters? Who is structurally excluded? Platforms that don't address power reproduce existing inequalities.

5. **Scale without depth.** India's 25 million users, France's 1.9 million contributions -- impressive numbers that produced minimal change. Volume of input is meaningless without quality of deliberation and commitment to act.

6. **No feedback loop.** Citizens contribute -> silence -> they never come back. The systems that work (Porto Alegre in its prime, Estonia) create visible connections between participation and results.
